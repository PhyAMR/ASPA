13 A light introduction

Some general concepts are here introduced, without much rigor, to give a broad idea about how to think of statistical inference.

13.1 The basics: deductive logic and the Bayesian framework

Statistics is the discipline that concerns the collection, organization, analysis, interpretation, and presentation of data. The European Mathematical Society classifies statistics as a branch of applied mathematic 1, specifically a subclass of probability theory and stochastic processes. However, it is also seen as a distinct scientific discipline due to its unique focus on real-world uncertainty and data context, involving empirical data gathering and experimental design.

Probability theory is the mathematical foundation for statistics. It is concerned with the study of probability, that quantifies of how likely, or plausible, an event is to happen, or a proposition to be true. Why do we need to introduce probabilities or deal with statistics? Because any real-world phenomenon involves systems of which we can have only a partial knowledge. Therefore, situations we deal with in everyday life are not completely predictable: uncertainty cannot be eliminated, we need to model it. More specifically, we have to act under the following paradigm, the one of inductive logic: given that certain effects have been observed, what is (are) the underlying cause(s)? Intuitively, plausible reasoning should be based on adjusting our belief about something, based on the occurrence or non-occurrence of something. This contrasts with deductive logic: given a well defined cause, we can work out its consequences. It is used in pure mathematics or theoretical physics, in which we can derive many exact results starting from few axioms or fundamental deterministic laws plus initial conditions, respectively.

Bayesian probability is an interpretation of probability theory in which probabilities quantify degrees of rational belief about propositions, conditional on stated information. Formally, the probability

\[ P(A \mid I) \]

represents the degree of belief that proposition \(A\) is true, given background information \(I\). Bayesian probabilities apply to arbitrary propositions, including hypotheses and models. Importantly, they are always conditional on information and are updated when new information becomes available. Belief updating, say about a certain hypothesis \(H\), is governed by Bayes’ theorem,

\[ P(H \mid D, I)=\frac{P(D \mid H, I) P(H \mid I)}{P(D \mid I)} \tag{1} \]

which follows directly from the product rule of probability. Here \(D\) denotes data, which are the result of experiments. In this view, probability theory is not a theory of frequencies, but a calculus for consistent reasoning in the presence of uncertainty. It allows one to deal rigorously with problems like inference: we observe data \(D\), we wish to reason about hypotheses \(H\). Our conclusions are uncertain but must be logically consistent!

A framework for deductive reasoning under uncertainty must satisfy logical consistency, quantitative comparison of plausibility, coherent updating when new data arrive, and applicability to arbitrary propositions and models. Bayesian probability satisfies all of these requirements. Moreover, under mild and explicit assumptions, it can be shown that

Any system that extends Boolean logic to uncertain propositions in a consistent, quantitative, and general way must be equivalent to Bayesian probability theory.

In this precise sense, Bayesian probability is not merely one possible method of inference, but the unique deductive logic for reasoning from data under uncertainty. Alternative frameworks (frequentist statistics, likelihood methods, belief functions) either avoid assigning probabilities to hypotheses, abandon full deductive consistency, or restrict the scope of admissible questions.

It is Cox’s theorem to provide the formal justification for Bayesian probability as a logic of inference. It originated from the desire to determine the most general mathematical rules for manipulating degrees of plausibility assigned to propositions, under requirements of rational consistency. It is based on the following simple assumptions: denoting \(w(A \mid B)\) the plausibility of proposition \(A\) given \(B\),

  1. degrees of plausibility can be represented by real numbers;
  2. if \(A\) is more plausible than \(B\), then \(w(A \mid I)>w(B \mid I)\) (ordering consistency);
  3. if a conclusion can be reached by different chains of reasoning, the assigned plausibility must be the same (logical consistency);
  4. the plausibility of the conjunction \(A B\) depends only on \(w(A \mid I)\) and \(w(B \mid A, I)\), and the plausibility of the negation \(\bar{A}\) (also called complement) depends only on the plausibility of the proposition itself.

From these assumptions, one can show that there exists a monotonic regraduation of \(w\) (a change of numerical scale) such that the regraduated plausibility \(P\) satisfies the sum and product rule, respectively,

\[ P(A \mid I)+P(\bar{A} \mid I)=1, \quad P(A, B \mid I)=P(A \mid I) P(B \mid A, I) \tag{2} \]

together with the fact that certain truth is represented by \(P(A \mid B)=1\). Here, the vertical bar ‘\(\mid\)’ means ‘given’, so that all items to the right of this conditioning symbol are taken as being true, and the comma is read as the conjunction ‘and’.

Relations (2) are precisely the axioms of probability theory, from which Bayes’ theorem then follows as a corollary. Thus Bayesian probability is best understood as extended logic: ordinary deductive logic applied to situations where truth is not fully known.

13.2 Handwaving introduction to Bayesian inference tasks

All inference problems can be framed as “inferring unknown objects (parameters, models, functions, structures, futures, or actions) from data under uncertainty”. Different problems differ mainly in (1) what is unknown (2) what question is being asked (3) what notion of optimality is used.

In statistics and data analysis, parameter estimation, model comparison and prediction, are the three most familiar inference tasks - they are not the only ones, though.

  • Parameter estimation: given some data \(D\), for a specific model \(H\), with parameters \(\theta\), infer their values.
  • Model comparison (aka hypothesis testing): given some data \(D\) and a set of models \(\left\{H_{j}\right\}\), find out which one is best supported by data.
  • Prediction: given a model \(H\), inferred from the data \(D\), predict new data at some new location (in the parameter space or time).

Within the Bayesian framework, that we will study, the two problems of parameter estimation and model comparison are formally the same: model comparison is basically estimation on a discrete parameter space.

The central idea is that we always use Bayes’ rule to get the distribution of an unknown random variable based on the observation of an other random variable:

\[ \underbrace{p(\theta \mid x)}_{\text {posterior }} \propto \underbrace{p(x \mid \theta)}_{\text {likelyhood }} \times \underbrace{p(\theta)}_{\text {prior }} \tag{3} \]

In the jargon of inference, we get the posterior (prob. of the unknown given the data) in terms of the prior (any previous belief we have on the unknown) and the likelyhood (the prob. of observed data given the unknown). As we see, and very differently from the frequentist approach, we consider the unknowns, e.g. the model’s parameter, as random variable to which we are able to associate a certain distribution, i.e. the posterior. Equation (3) is going to be our central instrument, to be applied in different situations depending on the specificities of the inference problem, such as, whether \(x\) and \(\theta\) are continuous or discrete variables.

Later, if we desire to extract a single numerical estimate of the unknown random variables, i.e. a point estimate, there are different options, each satisfying different optimality conditions. We will see for example that the estimator may be defined by the max of the posterior (MAP), or its mean (LMS), inter alia.

13.3 The practical machinery of inference

In practice, Bayesian inference requires computing integrals of the form

\[ \mathbb{E}[f(\theta) \mid D] \equiv \int f(\theta) p(\theta \mid D) d \theta \tag{4} \]

(that is a conditional expectation value), as well as marginal distributions, credible intervals, etc. These integrals are typically high-dimensional and analytically intractable. We will see that Monte Carlo methods provide a general numerical strategy to evaluate them. The Monte Carlo principle is based on the following idea: if \(\theta_{1}, \ldots, \theta_{N}\) are samples drawn from the posterior \(p(\theta \mid D)\), then the approximation

\[ \int f(\theta) p(\theta \mid D) d \theta \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f\left(\theta_{i}\right), \quad \text { as } N \rightarrow \infty \tag{5} \]

Thus, Bayesian computation reduces to the problem of generating samples from the posterior distribution. In this sense, Monte Carlo methods are not optional numerical tools, but an integral part of modern Bayesian inference, especially when dealing with large dimensional parameter space.
