16  Monte Carlo Methods

Monte Carlo methods are a broad class of computational algorithms that rely on repeated random sampling to obtain numerical results. Bayesian inference defines probability distributions and Monte Carlo methods provide the computational machinery to explore them.

16.1 Inversion of the Cumulative Distribution Function

The inversion of the cumulative distribution function (CDF) is one of the most fundamental and exact methods for sampling from a probability distribution. If the CDF of a random variable \(X\)

\[ F(x)=P(X \leq x)=\int_{-\infty}^{x} p\left(x^{\prime}\right) d x^{\prime} \tag{156} \]

can be computed and inverted, sampling is achieved by

\[ x=F^{-1}(u), \text { where } U \text { is uniformly distributed in }[0,1] . \tag{157} \]

Here, the inverse of \(F\) is \(F^{-1}(u)=\inf \{x: F(x) \geq u\}\). The result is based on the following equalities 1

\[ P(X \leq x)=P\left(F^{-1}(U) \leq x\right)=P(U \leq F(x))=\int_{0}^{F(x)} d u=F(x) \tag{158} \]

where the last passage come from the fact that \(p_{U}(u)=1\) in the interval \([0,1]\).

In Bayesian Inference, this method allows for exact sampling from simple posteriors, direct computation of expectations and efficient inference in conjugate Bayesian models. It is clearly limited to distributions with analytically invertible CDFs.

16.2 Rejection Sampling

Before introducing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods, it is useful to discuss a conceptually simpler strategy for sampling from a target distribution: rejection sampling. Unlike MCMC, rejection sampling generates independent samples, at the price of potentially low efficiency in high dimensions.

Suppose we wish to sample from a target density \(\pi(x)\) (defined on a space \(\mathcal{X}\)), known up to normalization,

\[ \pi(x)=\frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{Z} \tag{159} \]

where \(Z=\int_{\mathcal{X}} \tilde{\pi}(x) d x\) is unknown or difficult to compute. The idea is to introduce a proposal density \(q(x)\) (with \(q(x)>0\) whenever \(\tilde{\pi}(x)>0\)) which is easy to sample from. The latter is defined such that there exists a constant \(M>0\)

\[ \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{q(x)} \leq M \quad \forall x \in \mathcal{X} \tag{160} \]

In essence, the constant \(M\) provides an envelope bounding the unnormalized target density. The ideal choice for \(M\) is

\[ M^{\star} \equiv \max _{x \in \mathcal{X}} \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{q(x)} \tag{161} \]

as it maximizes the acceptance probability (see later). The main problem is that in high dimensions the max is often extremely hard to be determined.

The rejection sampling algorithm then proceeds as follows:

  1. Draw \(x \sim q(x)\) after having chosen a proposal density \(q(x)\) that approximates the shape of \(\pi(x)\) and is easy to sample from (e.g., Gaussian, uniform on a bounded domain, etc., but then must have the same support!).
  2. Draw \(u \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)\).
  3. Accept \(x\) if

\[ u \leq \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M q(x)}, \quad \text { with } M \text { chosen as close as possible to the estimated } M^{\star} \tag{162} \]

otherwise reject and return to step 1, repeating until the desired number of accepted samples is obtained.

We can verify that the distribution of accepted samples is \(\pi(x)\). Let \(A\) denote the event that a proposed \(x\) is accepted. The joint density of proposing \(x\) and accepting it is

\[ q(x) \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M q(x)}=\frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M} \tag{163} \]

The total probability of acceptance is therefore

\[ P(A)=\int_{\mathcal{X}} \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M} d x=\frac{1}{M} \int_{\mathcal{X}} \tilde{\pi}(x) d x=\frac{Z}{M} \tag{164} \]

which is less than or equal to 1 thanks to (160) and the fact that \(\int_{\mathcal{X}} q(s)=1\). The density of \(x\) conditioned on acceptance is

\[ p(x \mid A)=\frac{\frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M}}{P(A)}=\frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{Z}=\pi(x) \tag{165} \]

Thus, the accepted samples are independent and distributed according to the desired target density. For efficiency, one therefore wishes to choose \(q(x)\) such that \(M\) is as small as possible, ideally close to \(M^{\star}\).

Because each accepted sample is independent, no burn-in is required and no autocorrelation is present (see later for MCMC). However, the computational cost per accepted sample is proportional to \(1 / P(A)=M / Z\). If \(M\) is much larger than \(Z\), the method becomes inefficient.

The main limitation of rejection sampling appears in high dimensions. If \(\pi(x)\) is concentrated in a small region of the support of \(q(x)\), then \(M\) must be large and the acceptance probability becomes exponentially small in the dimension. This so-called curse of dimensionality severely restricts the applicability of rejection sampling in complex Bayesian models. For this reason, while rejection sampling is conceptually simple and mathematically transparent, it is typically replaced in high-dimensional inference problems by Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods, which trade independence of samples for dramatically improved efficiency.

16.3 Markov Chains

When direct sampling methods such as CDF inversion are not available, one typically resorts to Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. These algorithms set up a random walk over the parameter space which explores the regions of high probability density. More precisely, they construct a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target probability distribution \(\pi(x)\) of interest. Samples generated along the chain can then be used to estimate expectations with respect to \(\pi(x)\).

A discrete-time Markov chain is a stochastic process \(\left\{X_{t}\right\}_{t \geq 0}\) characterized by the conditional probability

\[ P\left(X_{t+1}=x^{\prime} \mid X_{t}=x\right) \equiv T\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right) \tag{166} \]

where \(T\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)\) is the transition probability from \(x\) to \(x^{\prime} \neq x\). The defining property is the Markov property: the future depends only on the present state and not on the past history, namely

\[ P\left(X_{t+1}=x^{\prime} \mid X_{t}=x, X_{t-1}=y, \ldots, X_{0}=z\right)=P\left(X_{t+1}=x^{\prime} \mid X_{t}=x\right)=T\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right), \tag{167} \]

The goal of MCMC is to design transition probabilities \(T\) such that the target distribution \(\pi(x)\) is a stationary distribution of the chain. We can interpret \(t\) as an algorithmic time with unit timestep.

The evolution of the probability distribution \(p_{t}(x)=P\left(X_{t}=x\right)\) associated with a Markov chain can be written in the form of a master equation. In discrete space, i.e., \(x \in \mathbb{Z}\), if we introduce a probability vector \(\boldsymbol{p}_{t}=\left(p_{t}\left(x_{1}\right), p_{t}\left(x_{2}\right), \ldots, p_{t}\left(x_{N}\right)\right)\) the master equation reads

\[ \boldsymbol{p}_{t+1}=\boldsymbol{T} \boldsymbol{p}_{t} \tag{168} \]

where the transition matrix \(\boldsymbol{T}\) has elements

\[ T_{x x^{\prime}}=\left\{\begin{array}{cc} T\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right) & \text { if } x \neq x \\ 1-\sum_{y} T(x \rightarrow y) & \text { if } x=x^{\prime} \end{array}\right. \tag{169} \]

Note that the diagonal components are chosen so as to ensure probability conservation, \(\mathbf{1} \cdot \boldsymbol{p}_{t}=\sum_{i} p_{t}\left(x_{i}\right)=1\) for all \(t \geq 0\).

It is useful to consider the continuous-time version. Formally, one rescale time \(t \mapsto \tau\) so that the original time unit becomes small, i.e., \(t+1 \mapsto \tau+d \tau\). We end up with the evolution

\[ \boldsymbol{p}_{\tau+d \tau}=(\mathbf{1}+d \tau \boldsymbol{W}) \boldsymbol{p}_{t} \approx e^{d \tau \boldsymbol{W}} \boldsymbol{p}_{\tau} \quad \text { as } d \tau \rightarrow 0 \tag{170} \]

with the transition rate matrix \(\boldsymbol{W}\) having elements

\[ W_{x x^{\prime}}=\left\{\begin{array}{ll} w\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right) & \text { if } x \neq x, \\ -\sum_{y} w(x \rightarrow y) & \text { if } x=x^{\prime} . \end{array} \quad w\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right) \equiv \lim _{d \tau \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{d \tau} T\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right)\right. \]

From (170) and the Markov property, it follows that

\[ \boldsymbol{p}_{\tau}=e^{\tau \boldsymbol{W}} \boldsymbol{p}_{0} \tag{172} \]

and taking a time derivative

$$ {}= e^{} {0}= _{t} $ $

Therefore, the continuous-time evolution reads

\[ \frac{d}{d t} p_{t}(x)=\sum_{x^{\prime}}\left[p_{t}\left(x^{\prime}\right) T\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right)-p_{t}(x) T\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)\right] \tag{174} \]

The first term accounts for transitions into state \(x\), while the second term represents transitions out of \(x\). It is useful to introduce the probability current from \(x\) to \(x^{\prime}\)

\[ J_{t}\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)=p_{t}(x) T\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right) \tag{175} \]

so that the master equation takes the compact form

\[ \frac{d}{d t} p_{t}(x)=\sum_{x^{\prime}}\left[J_{t}\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right)-J_{t}\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)\right] \tag{176} \]

This expression has the structure of a continuity equation in probability space. A distribution \(\pi(x)\) is stationary if it does not evolve in time, i.e.

\[ \frac{d}{d t} \pi(x)=0 \quad \forall x \tag{177} \]

From the master equation, stationarity requires that, for each state \(x\), the total incoming probability current balances the total outgoing one,

\[ \sum_{x^{\prime}}\left[\pi\left(x^{\prime}\right) T\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right)-\pi(x) T\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)\right]=0 \tag{178} \]

This condition corresponds to zero net probability currents and is the defining property of a steady state. What we want is that the Markov chain in the long time converges to such stationary distribution, i.e.,

\[ \lim _{t \rightarrow \infty} p_{t}(x)=\pi(x) \tag{179} \]

for all initial distributions \(p_{0}\). The fundamental requirement is irreducibility. The Markov chain is irreducible if, for any pair of states \(x\) and \(x^{\prime}\), there exists a finite time \(t\) such that

\[ P\left(X_{t}=x^{\prime} \mid X_{0}=x\right)>0 \tag{180} \]

In other words, the state space consists of a single communicating class. Irreducibility prevents the dynamics from decomposing into disconnected regions, each with its own stationary distribution. When a Markov chain is irreducible, the stationary distribution is unique and the chain is said to be ergodic 2. In this case, convergence (179) holds.

In practical Bayesian inference, these conditions justify the use of a burn-in period, during which early samples—still influenced by the initial condition \(\boldsymbol{p}_{0}\)—are discarded. After burn-in, time averages along the Markov chain provide consistent estimators of expectations with respect to the target posterior distribution. How long should the burn-in period be? Longer than the longest relaxation time of the chain. The latter can be precisely established looking at the eigenvalues \(\lambda_{k}\) of the matrix \(\boldsymbol{W}\) which set the inverse timescales of the relaxation. Indeed, expanding (172) into the left and right eigenvectors of \(\boldsymbol{W}\), \(\boldsymbol{l}_{k}\) and \(\boldsymbol{r}_{k}\) 3, defined by the equations

\[ \boldsymbol{W} \boldsymbol{r}_{k}=\lambda_{k} \boldsymbol{r}_{k}, \quad \boldsymbol{W}^{T} \boldsymbol{l}_{k}=\lambda_{k} \boldsymbol{l}_{k} . \tag{181} \]

we obtain for the probability evolution

\[ \boldsymbol{p}_{\tau}=\sum_{k} c_{k} \boldsymbol{r}_{k} e^{\tau \lambda_{k}} \tag{182} \]

where \(c_{k}=\boldsymbol{l}_{k} \cdot \boldsymbol{p}_{0}\) is the projection of the initial probability on the left eigenvector \(k\). Given the specific form of the transition rate matrix (171), the Perron-Frobenius theorem establishes that an eigenvalue exists that is equal to zero, \(\lambda_{0}=0\), and all other eigenvalues have smaller (thus, negative) real parts, i.e., \(0=\operatorname{Re}\left(\lambda_{0}\right) \geq \operatorname{Re}\left(\lambda_{1}\right) \geq \ldots\). The right eigenvector corresponding to \(\lambda_{0}=0\) is thus the stationary probability \(r_{0}(x)=\pi(x)\) and the left eigenvector is flat \(l_{0}(x)=\) const (as a consequence of probability conservation). Hence, the spectral decomposition (182) becomes

\[ \boldsymbol{p}_{\tau}=\boldsymbol{\pi}+\sum_{k>0} \boldsymbol{r}_{k} e^{-\tau\left|\operatorname{Re}\left(\lambda_{k}\right)\right|} e^{-\mathrm{i} \tau \operatorname{Im}\left(\lambda_{k}\right)} c_{k} \tag{183} \]

For long times, the decay of the initial condition is given by

\[ \left|\boldsymbol{p}_{\tau}-\boldsymbol{\pi}\right| \underset{t \rightarrow \infty}{\approx} e^{-\tau\left|\operatorname{Re}\left(\lambda_{1}\right)\right|}, \tag{184} \]

which shows that convergence is attained for times \(t \gtrsim 1 /\left|\operatorname{Re}\left(\lambda_{1}\right)\right|\), where \(\lambda_{0}-\left|\operatorname{Re}\left(\lambda_{1}\right)\right|\) is called spectral gap 4. The timescale over which convergence occurs is called mixing time, here showed to be proportional to the inverse of the spectral gap of the transition rate matrix. Slow mixing, corresponding to a small spectral gap, leads to long autocorrelation times and poor sampling efficiency.

16.3.1 Detailed balance

A particularly important special case of stationary state is that of detailed balance, which requires that probability currents vanish pairwise,

\[ \pi(x) T\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)=\pi\left(x^{\prime}\right) T\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right) \quad \forall x, x^{\prime} . \tag{185} \]

Detailed balance is a sufficient condition for stationarity, but not a necessary one: stationary distributions may exist with non-zero circulating currents, analogous to non-equilibrium steady states in statistical physics. Detailed balance stationary distributions are Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions in equilibrium statistical mechanics.

When detailed balance holds, the Markov chain is said to be reversible. In this case, the stationary distribution can often be identified directly by inspection, since the condition above implies

\[ \frac{T\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)}{T\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right)}=\frac{\pi\left(x^{\prime}\right)}{\pi(x)} . \tag{186} \]

Most commonly used MCMC algorithms, such as the Metropolis-Hastings scheme, are explicitly constructed to satisfy detailed balance with respect to the target distribution \(\pi(x)\). It can be shown that (186) allows to define a similarity transformation that symmetrizes \(\boldsymbol{W}\). This means that under detailed balance conditions the eigenvalues \(\lambda_{k}\) are all real 5.

In Bayesian inference, enforcing detailed balance is conceptually appealing and technically convenient, as it guarantees convergence to the desired posterior distribution. At the same time, the analogy with equilibrium statistical mechanics highlights that MCMC with detailed balance samples configurations according to equilibrium weights, with the Monte Carlo time playing the role of an artificial dynamics.

Once the chain has reached stationarity, expectation values of observables \(A(x)\) can be estimated as time averages along the chain,

$$ A=A(x) p(x) d x {t=1}^{N} A(x{t}) $ $

where \(\left\{x_{t}\right\}\) are successive samples generated by the Markov chain after the burn-in period. Under the condition of ergodicity, this estimator converges to the correct expectation value as \(N \rightarrow \infty\).

In Bayesian inference, MCMC is particularly powerful because it allows sampling from posterior distributions of the form

$$ p(D) p(D ) p() $ $

even when the normalization constant (the evidence) is unknown or intractable. Since MCMC only requires evaluation of the posterior up to a multiplicative constant, it scales naturally.

16.4 Monte Carlo implementation of discrete-time Markov chains

So far we have discussed the evolution of the probability distribution \(p_{t}(x)\) under a transition matrix \(\boldsymbol{T}\). In practice, however, we do not evolve the full distribution: we generate a single stochastic trajectory

\[ x_{0} \rightarrow x_{1} \rightarrow x_{2} \rightarrow \ldots \]

whose statistics reproduce the master equation in expectation. This is the essence of a Monte Carlo implementation of a discrete-time Markov chain.

Let the state space be either discrete or continuous. Given a current state \(x_{t}\), the algorithm must generate a new state \(x_{t+1}\) according to the transition probabilities \(T\left(x_{t} \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)\). Formally, the Monte Carlo procedure consists of:

  1. Given \(x_{t}\), construct a probability distribution \(T\left(x_{t} \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)\) over possible next states.
  2. Draw a uniform random number \(u \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)\).
  3. Select \(x_{t+1}\) according to the cumulative distribution associated with \(T\left(x_{t} \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)\).

In discrete state spaces, step (3) amounts to selecting \(x_{t+1}=x^{\prime}\) with probability \(T\left(x_{t} \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)\). In continuous spaces, one typically decomposes the transition in two steps: proposing a candidate \(x^{\prime}\) from a proposal density \(q\left(x^{\prime} \mid x\right)\) and then accepting or rejecting it with a suitable probability. This leads to the Metropolis-Hastings construction.

16.4.1 Metropolis-Hastings algorithm

Suppose we wish to sample from a target density \(\pi(x)\) known up to normalization. The Metropolis-Hastings (MH) algorithm defines a transition probability of the form

$$ T(x x{})=q(x{} x) (x x^{}), $ $

where \(q\left(x^{\prime} \mid x\right)\) is a proposal distribution and \(\alpha\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)\) is an acceptance probability to be determined. We impose detailed balance that ensures (more than!) stationarity with respect to \(\pi(x)\)

$$ (x) q(x^{} x) (x x{})=(x{}) q(x x^{}) (x^{} x) . $ $

A sufficient choice is

$$ (x x^{})=(1, ) . $ $

Indeed, if the ratio inside the minimum is larger than one, the move is always accepted; otherwise, it is accepted with probability equal to that ratio. Substituting this form into the detailed balance condition verifies that

\[ \pi(x) T\left(x \rightarrow x^{\prime}\right)=\pi\left(x^{\prime}\right) T\left(x^{\prime} \rightarrow x\right), \]

so that \(\pi(x)\) is stationary.

The practical implementation of one MH step is therefore:

  1. Given \(x_{t}\), draw a proposal \(x^{\prime} \sim q\left(x^{\prime} \mid x_{t}\right)\). This is a distribution from which one can easily draw a candidate sample \(x^{\prime}\) for the next point in the chain given \(x\).
  2. Compute the acceptance ratio

\[ r=\frac{\pi\left(x^{\prime}\right) q\left(x_{t} \mid x^{\prime}\right)}{\pi\left(x_{t}\right) q\left(x^{\prime} \mid x_{t}\right)} \]

  1. Draw \(u \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)\). If \(u<\min (1, r)\), set \(x_{t+1}=x^{\prime}\), otherwise set \(x_{t+1}=x_{t}\).

The possibility of rejection is crucial: it modifies the diagonal elements of the transition matrix so as to guarantee probability conservation and detailed balance. A particularly common case is that of a symmetric proposal (a Gaussian, for example),

\[ q\left(x^{\prime} \mid x\right)=q\left(x \mid x^{\prime}\right) \]

so that the acceptance probability simplifies to

$$ (x x^{})=(1, ) $ $

which is the original Metropolis algorithm.

16.4.2 Convergence diagnostics and autocorrelation

Even when irreducibility and aperiodicity hold in principle, in practical computations one must verify that the chain has effectively reached stationarity and that samples are sufficiently decorrelated. One simple way is to run the algorithm many times from different initial points in the parameter space.

$$ C_{A}()=A(x_{t}) A(x_{t+})-A^{2} $ $

\[ \bar{A}=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{t=1}^{N} A\left(x_{t}\right) \]

A practical criterion for convergence is that \(\rho_{A}(\tau)\) decays rapidly to zero compared to the total length of the chain.

16.4.3 The independence sampler

A particularly important special case of the Hastings construction is obtained when the proposal distribution is chosen independently of the current state,

$$ q(x^{} x)=q(x^{}) $ $

In this case the proposal step is no longer a local move but consists simply of drawing a fresh candidate \(x^{\prime}\) from a fixed density \(q\), regardless of the present configuration \(x_{t}\). The Hastings acceptance ratio becomes

$$ r= $ $

This algorithm is known as the independent Metropolis-Hastings algorithm (or independence sampler). Although proposals are independent draws from \(q\), successive accepted states are still correlated because acceptance depends on the current state through the factor \(q(x)\) in the ratio above.

The independence sampler is closely related to rejection sampling. In rejection sampling, one proposes \(x^{\prime} \sim q\left(x^{\prime}\right)\) and accepts with probability

$$ , $ $

where \(M\) is a global envelope constant such that \(\tilde{\pi}(x) \leq M q(x)\) for all \(x\).

In contrast, the independence sampler replaces the global bound \(M\) with a comparison to the current state \(x\),

$$ (x x^{})=(1, ) . $ $

One may therefore interpret the independence sampler as a rejection scheme in which the effective bound is updated dynamically along the Markov chain. Unlike rejection sampling, it does not require prior knowledge of a global constant \(M\), but it produces correlated samples. However, the performance of the independence sampler depends crucially on how well \(q(x)\) approximates \(\pi(x)\). If \(q(x) \approx \pi(x)\) so that the ratio \(\pi(x) / q(x)\) is nearly constant, then acceptance probabilities are close to one, autocorrelations are small, and the chain mixes rapidly, approaching the efficiency of independent sampling. On the other hand, if \(q(x)\) poorly approximates \(\pi(x)\)—for instance by underestimating its tails—then the ratio \(\pi(x) / q(x)\) can vary strongly across the state space. In this case, acceptance probabilities become small and the chain may remain trapped for long periods, leading to slow mixing and large autocorrelation times. This problem is particularly severe in high-dimensional spaces. A useful theoretical criterion is the following. If there exists a finite constant \(C>0\) such that

$$ (x) C q(x) x $ $

then the independence sampler is uniformly ergodic, and the spectral gap is bounded away from zero. This condition is formally analogous to the envelope condition required in rejection sampling and again highlights the close relationship between the two methods.

In Bayesian inference, the independence sampler is effective when a good global approximation to the posterior is available, for example from a Laplace approximation or a variational approximation. In such cases one may use the approximation as proposal density \(q(x)\). However, when the posterior distribution is highly concentrated, strongly correlated, or high-dimensional, constructing a proposal \(q(x)\) that globally approximates \(\pi(x)\) is often difficult. In those situations, local proposal mechanisms (such as Metropolis) typically yield better performance.

16.5 Gibbs sampling

In Bayesian inference one is often interested in expectations with respect to a high-dimensional posterior distribution

\[ p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid D), \quad \boldsymbol{\theta}=\left(\theta_{1}, \ldots, \theta_{d}\right) \]

such as

\[ \mathbb{E}[f(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \mid D]=\int f(\boldsymbol{\theta}) p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid D) d \boldsymbol{\theta} \]

Direct evaluation of these integrals or direct sampling from the joint posterior is typically infeasible when \(d\) is large or the posterior has a complicated structure.

Gibbs sampling is a MCMC method that generates samples from the joint posterior using only the full conditional distributions of each parameter. The construction of the Markov chain requires that for each component \(\theta_{i}\) the conditional distribution

\[ p\left(\theta_{i} \mid \theta_{-i}, D\right), \quad \theta_{-i} \equiv\left(\theta_{1}, \ldots, \theta_{i-1}, \theta_{i+1}, \ldots, \theta_{d}\right) \]

is known and can be sampled from directly. The Gibbs sampler constructs a Markov chain by sequentially updating each coordinate from its full conditional distribution while keeping the other coordinates fixed. The algorithm proceeds as follows:

  1. Initialize by choosing an initial state

\[ \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(0)}=\left(\theta_{1}^{(0)}, \ldots, \theta_{d}^{(0)}\right) \]

  1. For \(t=1,2, \ldots\), update each component sequentially:

\[ \begin{aligned} \theta_{1}^{(t)} & \sim p\left(\theta_{1} \mid \theta_{-1}^{(t-1)}, D\right) \\ \theta_{2}^{(t)} & \sim p\left(\theta_{2} \mid \theta_{-2}^{(t-1)}, D\right) \\ & \vdots \\ \theta_{d}^{(t)} & \sim p\left(\theta_{d} \mid \theta_{-d}^{(t-1)}, D\right) \end{aligned} \]

Each full sweep over all coordinates defines one transition \(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t-1)} \longrightarrow \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}\), whose corresponding transition operator can be written as the composition

\[ T=T_{d} \circ T_{d-1} \circ \cdots \circ T_{1} \]

where \(T_{i}\) denotes the update acting only on coordinate \(i\).

To show that the joint posterior \(p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid D)\) is invariant under Gibbs updates, consider a single-coordinate update \(T_{i}\). Its transition kernel reads

$$ T_{i}( ^{})=p({i}^{} {-i}, D) ({-i}^{}-{-i}) $ $

where \(\delta\) enforces that all other components remain unchanged. We verify detailed balance with respect to the joint posterior, starting from

$$ p( D) T_{i}( ^{})=p({i}, {-i} D) p({i}^{} {-i}, D) ({-i}^{}-{-i}) $ $

Using the factorization

\[ p\left(\theta_{i}, \theta_{-i} \mid D\right)=p\left(\theta_{i} \mid \theta_{-i}, D\right) p\left(\theta_{-i} \mid D\right) \]

we obtain

$$ \[\begin{align*} p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid D) T_{i}\left(\boldsymbol{\theta} \rightarrow \boldsymbol{\theta}^{\prime}\right) & =p\left(\theta_{i} \mid \theta_{-i}, D\right) p\left(\theta_{-i} \mid D\right) p\left(\theta_{i}^{\prime} \mid \theta_{-i}, D\right) \delta\left(\theta_{-i}^{\prime}-\theta_{-i}\right) \\ & =p\left(\theta_{i} \mid \theta_{-i}^{\prime}, D\right) \underbrace{p\left(\theta_{-i}^{\prime} \mid D\right) p\left(\theta_{i}^{\prime} \mid \theta_{-i}^{\prime}, D\right)}_{p\left(\theta_{i}^{\prime}, \theta_{-i}^{\prime} \mid D\right)} \delta\left(\theta_{-i}^{\prime}-\theta_{-i}\right) \\ & =p\left(\theta_{i}^{\prime}, \theta_{-i}^{\prime} \mid D\right) \underbrace{p\left(\theta_{i} \mid \theta_{-i}^{\prime}, D\right) \delta\left(\theta_{-i}^{\prime}-\theta_{-i}\right)}_{T_{i}\left(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\prime} \rightarrow \boldsymbol{\theta}\right)} \tag{206-208} \end{align*}\] $ $

which is the detailed balance condition

\[ p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid D) T_{i}\left(\boldsymbol{\theta} \rightarrow \boldsymbol{\theta}^{\prime}\right)=p\left(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\prime} \mid D\right) T_{i}\left(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\prime} \rightarrow \boldsymbol{\theta}\right) \]

Thus each coordinate update satisfies detailed balance, and since a composition of reversible kernels with the same invariant distribution remains invariant, the full Gibbs transition kernel leaves \(p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid D)\) invariant. Under the usual assumptions of irreducibility and aperiodicity, the chain is ergodic, and therefore

\[ \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)} \sim p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid D) \quad \text { for } t \rightarrow \infty \]

Gibbs sampling can be viewed as a special case of the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm. Indeed, updating coordinate \(i\) corresponds to choosing the proposal

\[ q\left(\theta_{i}^{\prime} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}\right)=p\left(\theta_{i}^{\prime} \mid \theta_{-i}, D\right) \delta\left(\theta_{-i}^{\prime}-\theta_{-i}\right) \]

that is, the proposal is exactly the conditional distribution of the target. Using the same chain of equalities employed to prove detailed balance, we show that the acceptance ratio becomes

\[ r=1, \]

so that every proposal is accepted. Gibbs sampling is therefore an MH algorithm with acceptance probability equal to one. When the full conditional distributions are not available in closed form, one may embed Metropolis steps inside the coordinate updates. This strategy is known as Metropolis-within-Gibbs.

Although Gibbs updates are always accepted, successive samples remain correlated. In particular, if components of \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) are strongly correlated under the posterior, updating them one at a time may lead to slow exploration of the joint space. Autocorrelation functions of observables and integrated autocorrelation times provide practical diagnostics of convergence, exactly as in the general MCMC case.

Gibbs sampling is thus conceptually simple (no proposal tuning parameters are required) and easy to implement (acceptance probability is identically one), particularly effective in hierarchical and conjugate Bayesian models, where conditional distributions take standard forms. As a major drawback, it requires that all full conditional distributions are known and directly samplable, mixing is slow when parameters are strongly correlated. Additionally, performance deteriorates in high-dimensional or multimodal problems.

16.6 Importance Sampling

In Bayesian inference we are interested in expectations with respect to a posterior distribution

\[ \pi(\theta) \equiv p(\theta \mid D) \]

such as

\[ \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[f(\theta)]=\int f(\theta) \pi(\theta) d \theta \]

When direct sampling from \(\pi(\theta)\) is difficult, but evaluation of the unnormalised posterior

\[ \tilde{\pi}(\theta)=p(D \mid \theta) p(\theta) \]

is possible, importance sampling provides an alternative Monte Carlo approximation method.

Let \(q(\theta)\) be a proposal density such that

\[ \operatorname{supp}(\pi) \subseteq \operatorname{supp}(q), \quad q(\theta)>0 \text { whenever } \pi(\theta)>0 \]

Then we may rewrite

$$ _{}[f()]=f() w() q() d $ $

with the importance weight

\[ w(\theta) \equiv \frac{\pi(\theta)}{q(\theta)} \]

If \(\theta_{i} \sim q(\theta)\) independently for \(i=1, \ldots, N\), then

$$ {N}= {i=1}^{N} f({i}) w({i}) $ $

is an unbiased estimator of \(\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[f(\theta)]\) provided \(\pi\) is known in normalized form.

However, in Bayesian applications, the posterior is typically known only up to normalisation:

\[ \pi(\theta)=\frac{\tilde{\pi}(\theta)}{Z}, \quad Z=\int \tilde{\pi}(\theta) d \theta \]

Since \(Z\) is unknown, we use self-normalised importance sampling. Defining unnormalised weights

\[ \tilde{w}_{i}=\frac{\tilde{\pi}\left(\theta_{i}\right)}{q\left(\theta_{i}\right)}, \quad \theta_{i} \sim q \]

the estimator becomes

$$ _{N}= $ $

This estimator is consistent but no longer unbiased, since it is a ratio of random variables (so its expectation does not coincide with the ratio of expectations!), but by the law of large numbers,

\[ \hat{I}_{N} \underset{N \rightarrow \infty}{\longrightarrow} \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[f(\theta)] \]

The variance of the basic (non-self-normalised) estimator is

\[ \operatorname{Var}\left(\hat{I}_{N}\right)=\frac{1}{N} \operatorname{Var}_{q}(f(\theta) w(\theta)) \]

Efficiency therefore depends critically on how variable the weights are. The optimal proposal, in the sense of zero variance for estimating a fixed expectation, is

\[ q^{\star}(\theta) \propto|f(\theta)| \pi(\theta) \]

which is typically unavailable in practice. A useful diagnostic is the effective sample size (ESS):

\[ \mathrm{ESS}=\frac{\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \tilde{w}_{i}\right)^{2}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \tilde{w}_{i}^{2}} \]

If the weights are highly uneven, \(\mathrm{ESS} \ll N\), indicating severe weight degeneracy.

In practice, for good performance one requires that:

  • \(q\) should approximate the posterior \(\pi\);
  • The tails of \(q\) must be at least as heavy as those of \(\pi\), otherwise the variance of the estimator may be infinite;
  • In high dimensions, \(q\) must match the geometry (correlations, scale, and support) of \(\pi\).

If \(\sup _{\theta} \frac{\pi(\theta)}{q(\theta)}=\infty\), the variance of the estimator may be infinite and the method unreliable.

Note that importance sampling can also estimate the evidence

\[ Z=\int \tilde{\pi}(\theta) d \theta=\int \frac{\tilde{\pi}(\theta)}{q(\theta)} q(\theta) d \theta \]

via the estimator

\[ \hat{Z}=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\tilde{\pi}\left(\theta_{i}\right)}{q\left(\theta_{i}\right)}, \quad \theta_{i} \sim q \]

In moderate to high dimensions, importance sampling often suffers from weight degeneracy: a small number of samples dominate the weight mass. This phenomenon arises because probability mass in high dimensions concentrates in thin regions, making it difficult for a fixed proposal distribution to approximate the posterior well. As dimension increases, the variance of the weights typically grows exponentially unless \(q\) is extremely well tuned. This curse of dimensionality is one of the main motivations for Markov chain Monte Carlo methods, which explore the posterior locally rather than relying on global reweighting.

16.7 Simulated Annealing

Simulated annealing is a stochastic optimisation method inspired by thermodynamic cooling. In Bayesian inference, it is primarily used to approximate the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimator rather than to compute posterior expectations. Let us use again the shorthand \(\pi(\theta)=p(\theta \mid D)\) to denote the posterior distribution. Introduce a temperature parameter \(T>0\) and define the tempered distribution

$$ {T}()=, Z{T}=()^{1 / T} d $ $

Writing the posterior in exponential form, thus exploiting the analogy equilibrium statistical mechanics,

\[ \pi(\theta)=\exp (-E(\theta)) \]

the tempered distribution becomes

\[ \pi_{T}(\theta)=\frac{\exp (-E(\theta) / T)}{Z_{T}} \]

Thus \(E(\theta)\) plays the role of an energy function and \(T\) acts as temperature in the Boltzmann distribution.

As \(T \rightarrow 0\), the tempered distribution peaks on its maximum

\[ \pi_{T}(\theta) \longrightarrow \delta\left(\theta_{\mathrm{MAP}}-\theta\right), \quad \theta_{\mathrm{MAP}}=\arg \max _{\theta} \pi(\theta)=\arg \min _{\theta} E(\theta) \]

More precisely, if the distribution is multimodal, the distribution concentrates on the global minimisers of \(E\), under mild regularity conditions. Therefore simulated annealing transforms a sampling problem into a sequence of increasingly concentrated distributions whose limit identifies the MAP estimator.

In practice, one constructs a sequence of temperatures

\[ T_{0}>T_{1}>\cdots>T_{K} \rightarrow 0 \]

At each temperature \(T_{k}\), one runs a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is \(\pi_{T_{k}}\), typically using a Metropolis-Hastings transition with acceptance probability

$$ _{T}(, {})=(1,(){1 / T} )=(1, (-) ) . $ $

For large \(T\), uphill moves \(\left(E\left(\theta^{\prime}\right)>E(\theta)\right)\) are frequently accepted, allowing the chain to escape local minima of \(E(\theta)\). As \(T\) decreases, the algorithm becomes increasingly greedy.

Simulated annealing can be viewed as a non-homogeneous MCMC method: at each fixed temperature, the Markov kernel leaves \(\pi_{T}\) invariant, but the target distribution itself changes over time. Unlike standard MCMC, whose goal is to sample from a fixed posterior to approximate expectations, simulated annealing intentionally drives the chain toward a degenerate limit distribution concentrated at the MAP estimate.

In Bayesian Inference Simulated annealing is used for:

  • global optimization and MAP estimation,
  • escaping local modes in highly multimodal posteriors,
  • constructing good initial states for subsequent MCMC runs,
  • approximating posterior modes in large-scale models.

16.7.1 Optimal cooling schedule

The behavior of simulated annealing depends critically on the cooling schedule. Classical convergence results show that if \(T_{k}\) decreases sufficiently slowly, for instance,

\[ T_{k} \sim \frac{c}{\log k} \]

under suitable assumptions, the algorithm converges to the set of global optima with probability one. Such logarithmic cooling schedule arises from the exponential scaling of escape times from local minima, a phenomenon analogous to the Arrhenius law in statistical physics.

Suppose the Markov chain is trapped in a local minimum of \(E\) that is separated from a deeper minimum by an energy barrier of height \(\Delta E>0\). At temperature \(T\), a Metropolis proposal that increases the energy by \(\Delta E\) is accepted with probability approximately

\[ p_{T} \approx \exp \left(-\frac{\Delta E}{T}\right) . \]

Such probability of crossing the barrier at one steps is the same exponential factor that governs thermally activated escape processes in statistical mechanics. Then the expected waiting time before escape is of order

\[ \tau(T) \approx \frac{1}{p_{T}} \approx \exp \left(\frac{\Delta E}{T}\right) \]

showing that escape times grow exponentially as temperature decreases. Now consider a decreasing temperature schedule \(T_{k}\). To guarantee convergence to the global minimum, the chain must retain the ability to escape local minima infinitely often as \(k \rightarrow \infty\). Assume the logarithmic schedule, the barrier-crossing probability at iteration \(k\) behaves as

\[ p_{T_{k}} \approx \exp \left(-\frac{\Delta E}{T_{k}}\right)=\exp \left(-\frac{\Delta E}{c} \log k\right)=k^{-\Delta E / c} \]

For the chain to escape a local minimum with probability one, the total number of escape opportunities must be infinite 6. This requires

\[ \infty=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} p_{k} \approx \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k^{-\Delta E / c} \]

The series diverges if and only if

\[ \frac{\Delta E}{c} \leq 1, \quad \text { that is, } \quad c \geq \Delta E \]

Therefore, if

\[ T_{k} \geq \frac{c}{\log k} \quad \text { with } \quad c>\Delta^{*} \]

where \(\Delta^{*}\) is the maximal depth of local minima, the algorithm converges to the global optimum with probability one under suitable regularity assumptions. Logarithmic cooling is therefore the slowest schedule that compensates

for the exponential growth of metastable escape times. However, such logarithmic cooling is typically too slow for practical use. In applications, geometric schedules are common:

\[ T_{k+1}=\gamma T_{k}, \quad 0<\gamma<1 . \]

Faster schedules such as the geometric cooling typically cause the chain to freeze in suboptimal local minima, while logarithmic cooling preserves the possibility of eventual barrier crossing. There is therefore a trade-off between theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility.
