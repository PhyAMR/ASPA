14  Inference Problems

Here we explore the different inference tasks, also presenting short comparisons between Bayesian and inference approaches. In frequentist inference, probability refers to long-run frequencies under repeated sampling. Parameters are fixed but unknown, data are random. In Bayesian inference, probability quantifies uncertainty or degree of belief. Parameters (or models) are treated as random variables.

14.1 Model comparison

In Bayesian inference, a specific hypothesis \(H\) is identified with a probabilistic model for the data \(D\). We assign a prior probability \(P(H)\) to each competing hypothesis.

Let \(\left\{H_{0}, H_{1}\right\}\) be two mutually exclusive and exhaustive hypotheses, so that

\[ P\left(H_{0}\right)+P\left(H_{1}\right)=P\left(H_{0} \cup H_{1}\right)=P(\Omega)=1 \tag{59} \]

(and the same holds for the conditionals \(P\left(H_{i} \mid D\right)\)) each of which specifies a likelihood \(P\left(D \mid H_{i}\right)\). Note that, if the model depends on parameters \(\theta\), then

\[ P(D \mid H)=\int P(D \mid \theta, H) P(\theta \mid H) d \theta \]

This quantity is called the marginal likelihood or model evidence. Hereafter, to illustrate the theory, we stick to the simplest case of two competing models without parameters (like ‘true’ or ‘false’).

Applying Bayes’ theorem to the hypotheses (treated as a random variable that can take only two values) gives

\[ P\left(H_{i} \mid D\right)=\frac{P\left(D \mid H_{i}\right) P\left(H_{i}\right)}{P(D)} \tag{60} \]

where the normalization is

\[ P(D)=P\left(D \mid H_{0}\right) P\left(H_{0}\right)+P\left(D \mid H_{1}\right) P\left(H_{1}\right) \tag{61} \]

The posterior odds ratio \(R\) then reads,

\[ R \equiv \frac{P\left(H_{1} \mid D\right)}{P\left(H_{0} \mid D\right)}=\frac{P\left(D \mid H_{1}\right)}{P\left(D \mid H_{0}\right)} \frac{P\left(H_{1}\right)}{P\left(H_{0}\right)}=\mathrm{BF} \frac{P\left(H_{1}\right)}{P\left(H_{0}\right)} \tag{62} \]

where the quantity

\[ \mathrm{BF} \equiv \frac{P\left(D \mid H_{1}\right)}{P\left(D \mid H_{0}\right)} \tag{63} \]

is called the Bayes factor and tell us the proportionality factor between the posterior odds and the prior odds. With this definition, we can rewrite 60, for the posterior as

\[ P\left(H_{0} \mid D\right)=\frac{1}{1+R}, \quad P\left(H_{1} \mid D\right)=\frac{1}{1+\frac{1}{R}} \tag{64} \]

We say that \(H_{1}\) is more likely than \(H_{0}\) conditional on the data \(D\), if the odds ratio \(R>1\). Often times, statisticians rather report \(\mathrm{BS}>1\) as a criterion for \(H_{1}\) to be more likely than \(H_{0}\). The reason being that BS contains only objective information coming from data, discarding “subjectivity” from the priors.

14.1.1 Marginal likelihood and Occam’s razor

Suppose hypothesis \(H\) has parameter \(\theta \in \Theta\) with prior density \(\pi(\theta)\). Then

\[ P(D \mid H)=\int_{\Theta} P(D \mid \theta) \pi(\theta) d \theta \tag{65} \]

We assume that the likelihood \(P(D \mid \theta)\) is sharply peaked around its maximum \(\hat{\theta}\) and the prior \(\pi(\theta)\) is smooth on the scale of the likelihood peak. Applying Laplace’s approximation,

\[ P(D \mid H) \approx \underbrace{P(D \mid \hat{\theta})}_{\text {best fit }} \times \underbrace{\pi(\hat{\theta}) \Delta \theta_{\mathrm{eff}}}_{\text {Occam factor }} \tag{66} \]

where \(\Delta \theta_{\text {eff }}\) is the effective width of the likelihood peak. The Occam factor gives the fraction of parameter space that fits the data. Complex models spread prior mass over large parameter volumes, with only a small region that fits the data well, i.e. \(\pi(\hat{\theta}) \Delta \theta_{\text {eff }} \ll 1\). The integral automatically penalizes unused parameter space, according to Occam’s razor:

Even though a more flexible model (more parameters) can fit the data better at its optimal parameter value, its overall predictive performance may be worse because most of its parameter space does not explain the data well.

As a simple illustration, consider the two models

  • \(H_{0}: \theta=\theta_{0}\), with no free parameters,
  • \(H_{1}: \theta \in[a, b]\) with uniform prior

that lead to the posterior distributions, respectively,

\[ \begin{align*} & P\left(D \mid H_{0}\right)=P\left(D \mid \theta_{0}\right) \\ & P\left(D \mid H_{1}\right)=\frac{1}{b-a} \int_{a}^{b} P(D \mid \theta) d \theta \approx P(D \mid \hat{\theta}) \frac{\Delta \theta_{\mathrm{eff}}}{b-a} \tag{67-68} \end{align*} \]

By definition of max, \(P\left(D \mid \theta_{0}\right) \leq P(D \mid \hat{\theta})\), meaning that \(H_{1}\) can fit better the data. However, if \(\theta_{\text {eff }} \ll b-a\), we end up with \(P\left(D \mid H_{1}\right)<P\left(D \mid H_{0}\right)\). Hence, \(P\left(D \mid H_{1}\right)\) will be smaller than \(P\left(D \mid H_{0}\right)\) despite having a higher maximum likelihood.

Marginal likelihoods balance fit quality and model complexity, with Occam’s razor being a mathematical consequence of integration over parameters.

14.2 Parameter estimation

A generative model is a theoretical model that predicts observable data from a set of parameters. The simplest example of a generative model is a straight line:

\[ f(x ; a, b)=a+b x \tag{69} \]

where \(a\) and \(b\) are unknown parameters. Measurements do not exactly follow the model due to noise. We therefore write

\[ y=f(x ; a, b)+\varepsilon \tag{70} \]

where \(\varepsilon\) represents measurement noise. The statistical properties of \(\varepsilon\) are described by a measurement model, which may itself depend on additional parameters. Given a data set

\[ D=\left\{y_{j}, x_{j}\right\} \tag{71} \]

our goal is to infer the values of the parameters of the generative model.

We denote by \(\theta\) the full set of parameters, including both generative and measurement parameters. Bayesian inference aims to determine the posterior probability density function

\[ P(\theta \mid D, H), \tag{72} \]

where \(H\) represents the background hypotheses, including the the choice of the generative model and the choice of the measurement (noise) model.

Again, Bayes’ theorem adapted to this setup states

\[ P(\theta \mid D, H)=\frac{P(D \mid \theta, H) P(\theta \mid H)}{P(D \mid H)} . \tag{73} \]

The likelihood \(P(D \mid \theta, H)\) is the probability of obtaining the measured data assuming specific values of the parameters. For example, assume that each measurement of \(y\) at fixed \(x\) is affected by Gaussian noise with variance \(\sigma^{2}\). Then the probability of a single measurement is

\[ P(y \mid \theta, H)=\frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2 \pi}} \exp \left[-\frac{(y-f(x ; a, b))^{2}}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right] . \tag{74} \]

This expresses the fact that measurements are normally distributed around the true model value. In this case, \(\theta=(a, b, \sigma)\) combining both generative and measurement parameters.

The posterior probability \(P(\theta \mid D, H)\) is the probability density of the parameters given the observed data and background information. Conceptually, Bayesian inference is straightforward: we update our prior knowledge using the information contained in the data. The prior probability \(P(\theta \mid H)\) encapsulates all information available before observing the data. It reflects the reality that scientific reasoning is never based on data alone.

The quantity

\[ P(D \mid H)=\int P(D \mid \theta, H) P(\theta \mid H) d \theta \tag{75} \]

is called the evidence, which is obtained by marginalizing over the parameters. The evidence plays a crucial role in model comparison, normalization of the posterior and computation of expectation values. In simple cases the integral can be evaluated analytically. For most realistic problems, however, numerical techniques such as Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) are required.

14.3 Point estimation

In the Bayesian paradigm the posterior distribution is the key object that summarizes our belief about the parameters after we have analyzed the data. However, we may be interested to get a single value, known as point estimate, which is to serve as a “best guess” of an unknown population parameter

Let \(X\) denote the observed data and \(\theta\) an unknown parameter. In Bayesian inference, a point estimator \(\hat{\theta}(X)\) is a function of the observed data \(X\) (hence, itself a random variable).

It is defined as the argument minimizing the posterior expected loss:

\[ \hat{\theta}(x)=\arg \min _{a} \mathbb{E}[L(a, \theta) \mid X=x]=\arg \min _{a} \int L(a, \theta) p(\theta \mid x) d \theta \tag{76} \]

Different choices of the loss function \(L(a, \theta)\) lead to different optimal estimators. The loss function quantifies the cost of choosing \(a\) when the true parameter is \(\theta\).

14.3.1 MAP estimator

The MAP estimator selects the single most probable value of the parameter given the observed data.

Consider the \(0-1\) loss

\[ L(a, \theta)= \begin{cases}0, & a=\theta \\ 1, & a \neq \theta\end{cases} \tag{77} \]

This loss is strictly appropriate for discrete parameters, or for continuous parameters when interpreted in an infinitesimal neighborhood sense. Namely, the \(0-1\) loss function is a Kronecker delta in the discrete case, or a (smoothed) Dirac delta in the continuous case.

The posterior expected loss is

\[ \mathbb{E}[L(a, \theta) \mid x]=\sum_{\theta \neq a} L(a, \theta) p(\theta \mid x)+p(\theta=a \mid x) \underbrace{L(\theta, \theta)}_{=0}=1-p(\theta=a \mid x) . \tag{78} \]

Minimizing this quantity is equivalent to maximizing the posterior density, yielding the maximum a posteriori estimator:

\[ \hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{MAP}}=\arg \max _{\theta} p(\theta \mid x) \tag{79} \]

There are some caveats with MAP that need to be listed:

  • Ill-defined decision problem in continuous spaces. If \(\theta\) is continuously distributed, then

\[ p(\theta=a \mid x)=0 \quad \text { for all } a \]

so all actions incur the same posterior expected loss. As a result, the \(0-1\) loss does not define a meaningful optimization problem without further regularization. In continuous spaces, the MAP estimator can only be justified via a limiting procedure, for example by considering losses of the form

\[ L_{\varepsilon}(\theta, a)=\mathbf{1}_{|\theta-a|>\varepsilon} \]

and letting \(\varepsilon \rightarrow 0\). The resulting estimator depends on how this limit is taken. In this sense, MAP is a limiting, not intrinsic, estimator.

  • Lack of invariance under reparametrization. The \(0-1\) loss is not invariant under smooth changes of variables. For example, imagine that we estimated \(\theta\) by MAP and later re-parametrize to a \(\phi=g(\theta)\), for some smooth function \(g\). In general, \(g\left(\hat{\theta}_{\text {MAP }}\right) \neq \hat{\phi}_{\text {MAP }}\) where \(\hat{\phi}_{\text {MAP }}\) is the MAP estimator of \(\phi\) obtained as the maximum of the distribution \(p_{\phi}(\phi \mid x)=p_{\theta}(\theta \mid x)\left|\operatorname{det} \frac{d \theta}{d \phi}\right|\) with \(\theta=g^{-1}(\phi)\). The Jacobian determinant of the change of variable shifts the maximum unless it is constant, or varies slowly near the mode.

Consequently, the MAP estimator depends on the chosen parametrization, in contrast with estimators such as the posterior mean that transform covariantly.

  • Insensitivity to posterior geometry. The \(0-1\) loss distinguishes only whether the estimate equals the true parameter, ignoring the magnitude of errors. This causes the MAP estimator to depend solely on the local behavior of the posterior density near its maximum, discarding information about spread, skewness, or multimodality.

But if the posterior is unimodal and sharply peaked, MAP is well behaving since it is often close to mean/median, with the advantage of being computationally convenient.

14.3.2 LMS estimator: the posterior mean

Given the squared-error loss

\[ L(a, \theta)=(a-\theta)^{2} \tag{80} \]

the posterior expected loss is

\[ \begin{align*} \mathbb{E}\left[(a-\theta)^{2} \mid x\right]=\int(a-\theta)^{2} p(\theta \mid x) d \theta & =a^{2}+\mathbb{E}[\theta \mid x]^{2}-2 a \mathbb{E}[\theta \mid x] \\ & =(a-\mathbb{E}[\theta \mid x])^{2}+\operatorname{Var}(\theta \mid x) \tag{81-82} \end{align*} \]

This expression is minimized when the parameter \(a\) coincides with the posterior mean,

\[ \hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{LMS}}=\mathbb{E}[\theta \mid x] \tag{83} \]

which is called the least mean square (LMS) estimator. Hence, the posterior mean minimizes the expected squared estimation error.

There are some caveats with LMS that need to be listed:

  • Non-existence of the posterior mean. The LMS estimator is well-defined only if the posterior distribution has a finite first moment. Heavy-tailed posteriors or the use of improper priors may lead to divergent posterior means, even when the posterior itself is normalizable.
  • Sensitivity to posterior tails. Because squared loss penalizes large deviations quadratically, the posterior mean can be strongly influenced by small amounts of posterior mass in the tails. As a consequence, rare but extreme parameter values may dominate the estimate.
  • Poor representativeness for multimodal posteriors. For multimodal posterior distributions, the posterior mean may lie in regions of low posterior density, for instance between well-separated modes. In such cases, the LMS estimator may not correspond to any “typical” parameter value.
  • Violation of natural constraints. When the parameter space is constrained, the posterior mean may fail to respect these constraints (e.g. positivity, boundedness, or structural conditions), potentially yielding estimates that are not physically or statistically meaningful.
  • Computational sensitivity in high dimensions. In high-dimensional or complex models, accurate computation of posterior means can be challenging. Monte Carlo estimators of expectations may converge slowly and be sensitive to sampling error, particularly in the presence of heavy tails.

Despite these caveats, the LMS estimator remains a central tool in Bayesian inference due to its invariance properties, admissibility under squared loss, and full use of the posterior distribution.

14.3.3 Other common loss functions

For the loss

\[ L(a, \theta)=|a-\theta| \tag{84} \]

one can show that the optimal estimator is the posterior median. Indeed, for continuous variables we can differentiate the posterior expected loss

\[ \partial_{a} \mathbb{E}[L(a, \theta) \mid x]=\int \operatorname{sgn}(a-\theta) p(\theta \mid x) d \theta=\mathbb{P}(\theta>a)-\mathbb{P}(\theta<a) \tag{85} \]

and set the result to zero to obtain

\[ a \text { such that } \mathbb{P}(\theta>a \mid x)=\mathbb{P}(\theta<a \mid x) \tag{86} \]

which is satisfied if \(a\) is the median of the posterior.

Asymmetric loss functions lead to posterior quantiles as optimal estimators, allowing different penalties for over- and under-estimation.

14.3.4 The risk function

The loss function \(L(a, \theta)\) quantifies the cost of choosing an action \(a\) when the true parameter value is \(\theta\). To assess the overall performance of a point estimator \(\hat{\theta}(X)\), one introduces the risk function, defined as the expected loss 1.

In Bayesian inference, the parameter itself is modeled as a random variable. Conditioning on the observed data \(X=x\), one defines the posterior risk of an action \(a\) as

\[ \rho(a \mid x)=\mathbb{E}[L(a, \theta) \mid X=x]=\int L(a, \theta) p(\theta \mid x) d \theta \tag{88} \]

The Bayesian point estimator is then obtained by minimizing the posterior risk:

\[ \hat{\theta}(x)=\arg \min _{a} \rho(a \mid x) \tag{89} \]

This local, data-dependent optimization problem leads directly to the estimators discussed above (MAP, posterior mean, median, etc.), each corresponding to a specific choice of loss function.

One can then define the Bayes risk, obtained by averaging over all parameters value and data the loss function

\[ \mathcal{R} \equiv \int L(a, \theta) p(\theta, x) d \theta d x=\int p(x) d x \underbrace{\int L(a, \theta) p(\theta \mid x) d \theta}_{\rho(a, x)} \tag{90} \]

which can also be written as the posterior risk averaged over the data. An estimator that minimizes the Bayes risk is called a Bayes estimator. Under mild regularity conditions, minimizing the posterior risk pointwise in \(x\) yields a Bayes estimator, establishing the equivalence between posterior expected loss minimization and global Bayes optimality. Namely, if we ask which function \(\hat{a}(x)\) minimizes the functional \(\mathcal{R}\) we obtain

\[ 0=\frac{\delta \mathcal{R}}{\delta \hat{a}(x)}=\int p(x) \frac{\delta \rho(\hat{a}, x)}{\delta \hat{a}} d x \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \frac{\delta \rho(\hat{a}, x)}{\delta \hat{a}}=0 \tag{91} \]

Since \(p(x)>0\) we have concluded that the posterior risk must be minimized, which is precisely condition 89). We therefore conclude that the point estimator \(\hat{\theta}(x)\) that minimize the expected loss “pointwise” also minimize the risk “globally”.

14.4 Reliability of parameter estimation

Once the posterior distribution \(P(\theta \mid D, H)\) has been obtained, an important question arises: how reliable are the inferred parameter values? The answer depends strongly on the shape of the posterior distribution.

14.4.1 Peaked Posteriors and Gaussian Approximation

In many favorable situations, the posterior distribution is unimodal and is sharply peaked around the MAP estimate \(\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{MAP}}\), such that the mode does not differ much from the mean and the median. It is often convenient to work with the logarithm of the posterior,

\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta)=\log P(\theta \mid D, H) \tag{92} \]

which reaches its maximum at the same point \(\hat{\theta}_{\text {MAP }}\). Given that the posterior is sufficiently narrow, we can perform a Taylor expansion of \(\mathcal{L}(\theta)\) around \(\hat{\theta}_{\text {MAP }}\):

\[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) \simeq \mathcal{L}(\hat{\theta})-\frac{1}{2}(\theta-\hat{\theta})^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{H}(\theta-\hat{\theta}) \tag{93} \]

where \(\boldsymbol{H}\) is the Hessian matrix of second derivatives,

\[ H_{i j}=-\left.\frac{\partial^{2} \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_{i} \partial \theta_{j}}\right|_{\hat{\theta}}>0 \tag{94} \]

Under this approximation, the posterior becomes Gaussian \(P(\theta \mid D, H) \approx \mathcal{N}(\hat{\theta}, \boldsymbol{C})\) with covariance matrix \(\boldsymbol{C}=\boldsymbol{H}^{-1}\). The diagonal elements of \(\boldsymbol{C}\) provide estimates of the parameter variances,

\[ \sigma_{i}^{2}=C_{i i} \tag{95} \]

while the off-diagonal elements quantify correlations between parameters. One can then provide the final estimate for the vector if parameter \(\theta=\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{MAP}} \pm \operatorname{diag}(\boldsymbol{C})\).

14.4.2 Credible intervals

In many realistic inference problems, the posterior distribution is not well approximated by a Gaussian since it may be broad, very asymmetric, multimodal, or truncated by parameter boundaries. In such cases, quoting a single best-fit value and a symmetric error bar can be misleading.

A more appropriate characterization of parameter uncertainty is given by credible intervals. For a parameter \(\theta_{i}\), a credible interval \(\left[\theta_{i}^{-}, \theta_{i}^{+}\right]\)at credibility level \(\gamma\) satisfies

\[ \gamma \equiv \int_{\theta_{i}^{-}}^{\theta_{i}^{+}} P\left(\theta_{i} \mid D, H\right) d \theta_{i} \tag{96} \]

where \(P\left(\theta_{i} \mid D, H\right)\) is the marginal posterior for \(\theta_{i}\). Unlike frequentist confidence intervals (see later), credible intervals have a direct probabilistic interpretation: the parameter lies within the interval with probability \(\gamma\), given the data and the model.

Common choices include central credible intervals and highest posterior density (HPD) intervals. A central credible interval at credibility level \(\gamma\) is defined by excluding equal probability mass from both tails of the posterior distribution. Hence, the interval \(\left[\theta_{i}^{-}, \theta_{i}^{+}\right]\)satisfies

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\theta^{-}} p\left(\theta_{i}\right) d \theta_{i}=\int_{\theta^{+}}^{\infty} p\left(\theta_{i}\right) d \theta_{i}=\frac{1-\gamma}{2} \tag{97} \]

For a Gaussian posterior, the central credible interval coincides with the familiar \(\hat{\theta} \pm n \sigma\) interval, with \(\gamma \simeq 68 \%\) at \(n=1\), \(\gamma \simeq 95 \%\) at \(n=1.96\), and \(\gamma \simeq 99.7 \%\) at \(n=3\). For asymmetric or non-Gaussian posteriors, however, the resulting interval need not be symmetric around any point estimate.

A highest posterior density (HPD) interval at credibility level \(\gamma\) is defined as the region of parameter space containing probability mass \(\gamma\) with the highest posterior density. Formally, the HPD interval is given by

\[ \mathrm{HPD}_{\gamma}=\left\{\theta: p\left(\theta_{i}\right) \geq k_{\gamma}\right\} \tag{98} \]

where the threshold \(k_{\gamma}\) is chosen such that

\[ \int_{p\left(\theta_{i}\right) \geq k_{\alpha}} p\left(\theta_{i}\right) d \theta_{i}=\gamma \tag{99} \]

By construction, every point inside an HPD interval has a higher posterior density than any point outside. Among all regions with probability content \(\gamma\), the HPD interval has minimum width. For unimodal posterior distributions, the HPD region is a single connected interval, while for multimodal posteriors, it may consist of multiple disjoint intervals. In the special case of a symmetric unimodal distribution, the HPD and central credible intervals coincide.

The reliability of parameter estimates should always be assessed by inspecting the full posterior distribution, in particular checking for asymmetries, long tails, or verifying the validity of Gaussian approximations. When necessary, credible intervals should be reported rather than bare point estimates.

14.5 Comparing to frequentist parameter estimation

In the frequentist approach, model parameters are treated as fixed but unknown quantities. Randomness enters only through the data, which are assumed to be realizations of a random process governed by the true parameter values. The goal of parameter estimation is to construct an estimator, that is, a function of the data used to infer the unknown parameters.

Let \(\theta\) denote an unknown parameter and let

\[ D=\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\} \tag{100} \]

be a set of independent observations drawn from a probability distribution \(p(x \mid \theta)\). An estimator of \(\theta\) is a statistic

\[ \hat{\theta}=\hat{\theta}(D) \tag{101} \]

which is itself a random variable due to the randomness of the data.

A common construction principle is the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE), defined as

\[ \hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{MLE}}=\arg \max _{\theta} L(\theta) \tag{102} \]

where the likelihood function is

\[ L(\theta)=\prod_{i=1}^{N} p\left(x_{i} \mid \theta\right) \tag{103} \]

Equivalently, one may maximize the log-likelihood,

\[ \ell(\theta)=\sum_{i=1}^{N} \log p\left(x_{i} \mid \theta\right) \tag{104} \]

The bias of an estimator quantifies systematic deviation from the true parameter value:

\[ \operatorname{Bias}(\hat{\theta})=\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}]-\theta \tag{105} \]

where the expectation is taken over repeated realizations of the data drawn from \(p(x \mid \theta)\). An estimator is said to be unbiased if \(\operatorname{Bias}(\hat{\theta})=0\), and biased otherwise. Bias alone is not sufficient to assess estimator quality, since an unbiased estimator may still have large fluctuations.

The variance of an estimator measures its statistical uncertainty:

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\hat{\theta})=\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}])^{2}\right] \tag{106} \]

A commonly used global measure of estimator performance is the mean squared error (MSE),

\[ \operatorname{MSE}(\hat{\theta})=\mathbb{E}_{\theta}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta)^{2}\right]=\operatorname{Var}(\hat{\theta})+\operatorname{Bias}(\hat{\theta})^{2} \tag{107} \]

This decomposition highlights the trade-off between bias and variance.

For unbiased estimators, the variance is bounded from below by the Cramér-Rao lower bound

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq \frac{1}{I(\theta)} \tag{108} \]

where \(I(\theta)\) is the Fisher information,

\[ I(\theta)=\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p(x \mid \theta)\right)^{2}\right] \tag{109} \]

An unbiased estimator that attains the Cramér-Rao bound is called efficient. In many regular problems, the maximum likelihood estimator becomes asymptotically efficient as the sample size increases.

An estimator is said to be consistent if it converges in probability to the true parameter value as the number of data points grows:

\[ \hat{\theta}_{N} \xrightarrow[N \rightarrow \infty]{P} \theta \tag{110} \]

Consistency ensures that, with enough data, the estimator recovers the true parameter, regardless of its finite-sample bias or variance. Most maximum likelihood estimators are consistent under mild regularity conditions.

Unlike Bayesian inference, all the above properties accessing the optimality of the estimator are defined with respect to hypothetical repetitions of the experiment, rather than conditioning on the observed data.

Example: bias of the maximum likelihood estimator of the variance

Let \(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\mu, \sigma^{2}\right)\). The maximum likelihood estimators are

\[ \hat{\mu}=\bar{x}=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} x_{i}, \quad \hat{\sigma}^{2}=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2} \]

We want to show the classical result that the maximum likelihood estimator \(\hat{\sigma}^{2}\) is biased, with

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{\sigma}^{2}\right]=\frac{N-1}{N} \sigma^{2} . \]

Starting from the identity

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2}=\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(x_{i}-\mu\right)^{2}-N(\bar{x}-\mu)^{2} . \]

and taking expectations term by term, we obtain

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(x_{i}-\mu\right)^{2}\right]=N \sigma^{2} \]

since \(x_{i}-\mu \sim \mathcal{N}\left(0, \sigma^{2}\right)\), and

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[N(\bar{x}-\mu)^{2}\right]=N \operatorname{Var}(\bar{x})=\sigma^{2} \]

since \(\bar{x} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\mu, \sigma^{2} / N\right)\). Combining the two results yields

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2}\right]=(N-1) \sigma^{2} \]

Dividing by \(N\) concludes the proof:

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{\sigma}^{2}\right]=\frac{1}{N} \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2}\right]=\frac{N-1}{N} \sigma^{2} \]

The bias originates from estimating the mean \(\mu\) from the same data used to estimate the variance, effectively removing one degree of freedom. The bias vanishes as \(N \rightarrow \infty\), so \(\hat{\sigma}^{2}\) is a consistent estimator.

14.5.1 Confidence intervals vs credible intervals

In frequentist language, let \(X\) denote the observed data, modeled as a random variable with distribution \(p_{\theta}(X)\) depending on an unknown parameter \(\theta\) living in the parametric space \(\Theta\). A confidence interval is a random set

\[ C(X) \subset \Theta \]

since it is a function of the random data \(X\). The parameter \(\theta\) itself is treated as fixed but unknown.

A confidence interval procedure \(C(X)\) is said to have coverage probability \(1-\alpha\) if

\[ \mathbb{P}(\theta \in C(X))=1-\alpha \quad \text { for all } \theta \in \Theta . \tag{111} \]

The probability is taken with respect to the sampling distribution of the data \(X\), while the parameter \(\theta\) is held fixed. Coverage is thus a statement about repeated sampling: The experiment is repeated many times, each time new data \(X\) are generated from \(p_{\theta}(X)\), a new confidence interval \(C(X)\) is constructed. If the procedure has \(95 \%\) coverage, then in the long run, \(95 \%\) of the constructed intervals will contain the true value of \(\theta\).

Coverage does not imply the conditional probability statement

\[ \mathbb{P}(\theta \in C \mid X=x)=1-\alpha \]

Such a statement is meaningless in the frequentist framework, because the parameter \(\theta\) is not a random variable and probability statements are not conditioned on the observed data. Once the data \(x\) are observed, the interval \(C(x)\) either contains \(\theta\) or it does not.

As an example, take the normal distribution for the mean with known variance, namely, let \(X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\mu, \sigma^{2}\right)\) with known \(\sigma\). A \(1-\alpha\) confidence interval for \(\mu\) is

\[ C(X)=\bar{X} \pm z_{1-\alpha / 2} \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

where \(z_{1-\alpha / 2}\) is the \((1-\alpha / 2)\) quantile of the standard normal distribution 2. This interval satisfies the coverage property:

\[ \mathbb{P}\left(\mu \in \bar{X} \pm z_{1-\alpha / 2} \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)=1-\alpha . \tag{112} \]

In Bayesian inference, \(\theta\) is treated as a random variable with posterior distribution \(p(\theta \mid X)\). A Bayesian credible interval may or may not satisfy frequentist coverage. In special cases (e.g. conjugate priors, large samples), credible intervals may have approximate frequentist coverage (e.g. Bernstein-von Mises theorem). All in all, coverage is a property of the interval-producing procedure, not of a single realized interval.

14.6 Prediction

In Bayesian inference, all sources of uncertainty are represented probabilistically. Model parameters are treated as random variables, and inference proceeds by updating prior beliefs in light of observed data. A central object in this framework is the posterior predictive distribution, which governs predictions for future observations.

Prediction for a new observation \(x_{\text {new }}\) is obtained by marginalizing over the posterior distribution of the parameters:

\[ p\left(x_{\mathrm{new}} \mid x\right)=\int p\left(x_{\mathrm{new}} \mid \theta\right) p(\theta \mid x) d \theta \tag{113} \]

This distribution is referred to as the posterior predictive distribution. It is a genuine probability distribution on the space of future data, conditioned only on the observations \(x\), with the parameter \(\theta\) fully integrated out.

From a probabilistic standpoint, the predictive distribution follows directly from the joint model

\[ p\left(x_{\text {new }}, \theta \mid x\right)=\underbrace{p\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid \theta\right)}_{\text {likelihood calculated on new points }} \times \underbrace{p(\theta \mid x)}_{\text {posterior }}, \]

by marginalization over \(\theta\). No point estimate of the parameters is required at any stage.

A key conceptual feature of Bayesian inference is that uncertainty in the model parameters is automatically propagated to predictions. Since \(p(\theta \mid x)\) typically has nonzero variance, predictions for \(x_{\text {new }}\) incorporate both intrinsic data variability and parameter uncertainty.

To make this explicit, consider a model in which the conditional variance of \(x_{\text {new }}\) given \(\theta\) is finite. The total predictive variance satisfies the law of total variance,

\[ \operatorname{Var}\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid x\right)=\mathbb{E}_{\theta \mid x}\left[\operatorname{Var}\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid \theta\right)\right]+\operatorname{Var}_{\theta \mid x}\left[\mathbb{E}\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid \theta\right)\right] \]

The first term reflects irreducible noise in the data-generating process, while the second term quantifies uncertainty due to incomplete knowledge of the parameters. Both contributions are present automatically in the Bayesian predictive distribution. By contrast, predictive schemes based on point estimates \(\hat{\theta}(x)\) typically replace \(p\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid x\right)\) with \(p\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid \hat{\theta}\right)\), thereby discarding the second contribution. In the Bayesian framework, no such approximation is made: the predictive distribution remains valid even in regimes of limited data or weak identifiability.

More generally, Bayesian prediction is invariant under reparameterization and naturally extends to hierarchical and latent-variable models, where multiple layers of uncertainty are integrated out through successive marginalizations. This makes the predictive distribution the fundamental object of inference, rather than parameter estimates themselves.

14.7 Comparing to prediction in the frequentist paradigm

In the frequentist framework, prediction is formulated in terms of the sampling distribution of future observations, conditional on unknown but fixed model parameters. Since parameters are not treated as random variables, uncertainty about their values cannot be integrated out. Instead, prediction typically relies on point estimates and procedures designed to achieve correct long-run coverage under repeated sampling.

Let \(x=\left(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right)\) denote observed data drawn from a model \(p(x \mid \theta)\), with \(\theta \in \Theta\) fixed but unknown. A future observation \(x_{\text {new }}\) is assumed to be generated from the same conditional distribution \(p\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid \theta\right)\). A common frequentist approach to prediction is the plug-in principle 3, whereby \(\theta\) is replaced by an estimator \(\hat{\theta}(x)\), yielding the predictive distribution

\[ p_{\text {plug }}\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid x\right)=p\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid \hat{\theta}(x)\right) \]

This distribution is interpreted as an approximation to the true sampling distribution of \(x_{\text {new }}\) under the unknown parameter value. The validity of plug-in prediction is assessed indirectly through the sampling properties of \(\hat{\theta}\), such as consistency and asymptotic normality. In large samples, \(p\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid \hat{\theta}\right)\) typically converges to the true conditional distribution \(p\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid \theta\right)\), but for finite samples it neglects the uncertainty associated with estimating \(\theta\).

Rather than focusing on full predictive distributions, frequentist prediction often centers on the construction of prediction intervals. A prediction interval \(\mathcal{I}(x)\) for \(x_{\text {new }}\) is a data-dependent set satisfying the coverage condition

\[ \mathbb{P}_{\theta}\left(x_{\text {new }} \in \mathcal{I}(X)\right) \geq 1-\alpha \quad \text { for all } \theta \in \Theta \]

where the probability is taken with respect to the joint sampling distribution of the observed data \(X\) and the future observation \(x_{\text {new }}\).

The defining property of a frequentist prediction interval is therefore its long-run coverage: over repeated realizations of the experiment, a proportion at least \(1-\alpha\) of intervals constructed in this way will contain the future observation. This guarantee holds regardless of the realized value of \(\theta\), provided the model assumptions are satisfied. In practice, prediction intervals are often derived using asymptotic approximations. For example, if \(\hat{\theta}\) is asymptotically normal,

\[ \sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}-\theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\left(0, \sigma^{2}\right), \]

then an approximate prediction interval for \(x_{\text {new }}\) can be constructed by combining the variability of \(p\left(x_{\text {new }} \mid \theta\right)\) with the estimated sampling variability of \(\hat{\theta}\).

Unlike Bayesian credible intervals for predictions, frequentist prediction intervals are not interpreted as probability statements conditional on the observed data. Instead, their meaning is entirely operational: they control the frequency of correct predictions under repeated sampling.
