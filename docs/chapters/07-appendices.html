18 Appendix A: \(\sigma\)-Algebras

In probability theory, not every subset of the sample space \(\Omega\) can be consistently assigned a probability. A \(\sigma\)-algebra specifies which subsets of \(\Omega\) are regarded as admissible events, i.e. which questions about the outcome of an experiment are meaningful.

Definition. Let \(\Omega\) be a non-empty set. A collection \(\mathcal{F} \subseteq 2^{\Omega}\) is called a \(\sigma\)-algebra if it satisfies:

  1. Containment of the empty set:

\[ \varnothing \in \mathcal{F} ; \]

  1. Closure under complement: if \(A \in \mathcal{F}\), then

\[ \bar{A}=\Omega \backslash A \in \mathcal{F} \]

  1. Closure under countable unions: if \(\left\{A_{n}\right\}_{n=1}^{\infty} \subset \mathcal{F}\), then

\[ \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_{n} \in \mathcal{F} \]

From these properties it follows, via De Morgan’s laws, that \(\mathcal{F}\) is also closed under countable intersections.

Interpretation. Elements of a \(\sigma\)-algebra are called events. The defining properties ensure that:

  • if an event is meaningful, then its negation is meaningful;
  • if countably many events are meaningful, then the event that at least one of them occurs is also meaningful;
  • limits of sequences of events remain well-defined.

This logical stability is essential for defining probabilities and for taking limits.

Examples.

  • The trivial \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\{\varnothing, \Omega\}\), containing only impossible and certain events.
  • If \(\Omega\) is finite or countable, the power set \(2^{\Omega}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra.
  • On \(\Omega=\mathbb{R}\), the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra is generated by open intervals and is the standard choice for defining probability densities.

Relation to Probability Measures. A probability measure is a function

\[ \mathbb{P}: \mathcal{F} \rightarrow[0,1] \]

defined on a \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{F}\) and satisfying the Kolmogorov axioms. The \(\sigma\)-algebra determines which events can be assigned probabilities, while the probability measure assigns numerical values to them. In Bayesian inference, \(\sigma\)-algebras provide the rigorous mathematical foundation for defining random variables, conditioning, and posterior distributions.

19 Appendix B: Events as a Boolean Algebra

The operations on events introduced in probability theory are expressed in the language of set theory, but they can equivalently be rewritten in the language of Boolean algebra. The underlying mathematics is the same, only the point of view changes.

Events form a Boolean algebra. Given a sample space \(\Omega\) and a \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{F}\), the elements of \(\mathcal{F}\) (events), together with the operations

\[ A \cap B \quad (\mathrm{AND}), \quad A \cup B \quad (\mathrm{OR}), \quad A^{c} \equiv \bar{A} \quad (\mathrm{NOT}), \]

form a Boolean algebra. Thus, passing from set-theoretic notation to logical notation does not alter the structure, but only the language used to describe it.

19.1 Dictionary between set theory and Boolean algebra

Set-theoretic language Boolean-algebra language Meaning
Event \(A \subset \Omega\) Proposition \(A\) Statement about the outcome
\(A \cap B\) \(A \wedge B\) or \(A \cdot B\) Logical AND
\(A \cup B\) \(A \vee B\) or \(A+B\) Logical OR
\(A^{c}\) or \(\bar{A}\) \(\neg A\) Logical NOT
\(\varnothing\) (empty set) False Impossible event
\(\Omega\) (full space) True Certain event

In Boolean algebra, events are treated as truth values, and the operations obey logical rules rather than geometric ones.

Formal remark: Why probability uses set theory. Probability theory is formulated within measure theory, where probability measures act on sets, integrals require measurable sets, limits of sequences of events require closure under countable operations. Unfortunately, Boolean algebra alone is too weak to support these constructions. A \(\sigma\)-algebra is precisely a Boolean algebra that is closed under countable unions and intersections.

The bridge: indicator functions. The connection between Boolean logic and set-theoretic probability is provided by the indicator function

\[ \mathbf{1}_{A}(\omega)= \begin{cases}1, & \omega \in A, \\ 0, & \omega \notin A .\end{cases} \]

Under this correspondence, Boolean operations become algebraic identities:

\[ \mathbf{1}_{A \cap B}=\mathbf{1}_{A} \mathbf{1}_{B}, \quad \mathbf{1}_{\bar{A}}=1-\mathbf{1}_{A}, \quad \mathbf{1}_{A \cup B}=\mathbf{1}_{A}+\mathbf{1}_{B}-\mathbf{1}_{A} \mathbf{1}_{B} . \]

This correspondence explains why logical notation and algebraic products sometimes appear in probability formulas.

Interpretation in Bayesian inference. In Bayesian inference, events often represent logical statements such as hypotheses, model assumptions, or parameter constraints. Probabilities quantify degrees of belief in these statements, making the Boolean interpretation conceptually natural. Nevertheless, the set-theoretic formulation is essential to ensure mathematical consistency and to support conditioning, integration, and limits. Events in probability theory form a Boolean algebra, and the familiar set-theoretic operations are simply the concrete realization of logical operations within measure theory.

20 Appendix C: Hypothesis Testing in Frequentist Approach

In the frequentist framework, model comparison is formulated as a problem of hypothesis testing based on the sampling properties of observables. Probability is interpreted operationally, as arising from repeated realizations of the same experiment, while model parameters are treated as fixed but unknown quantities.

Let \(\left(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P}_{\theta}\right)\) denote a statistical model, where \(\theta \in \Theta\) is a vector of unknown parameters on which \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta}\) depends. A hypothesis is defined as a subset of the parameter space. In its simplest form, one considers exclusive hypotheses, i.e.,

\[ H_{0}: \theta \in \Theta_{0}, \quad H_{1}: \theta \in \Theta_{1}, \]

with \(\Theta_{0} \cap \Theta_{1}=\varnothing\) and \(\Theta_{0} \cup \Theta_{1} \subseteq \Theta\). In this setting, \(\theta\) is not a random variable, and no probability measure is assigned to \(\Theta_{0}\) or \(\Theta_{1}\). Instead, the goal is to assess whether the observed data are compatible with the assumption that the true (but unknown) parameter value lies in \(\Theta_{0}\). Different statistical models can be compared by embedding them into a common parametric family and interpreting each model as a hypothesis restricting the admissible parameter values.

Given an observation \(X \in \Omega\), inference is based on a measurable test statistic

\[ T: \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \]

chosen so that large (or small) values of \(T(X)\) indicate disagreement with the null hypothesis \(H_{0}\). The distribution of \(T(X)\) under \(H_{0}\),

\[ F_{T, 0}(t)=\mathbb{P}_{\theta}(T(X) \leq t), \quad \theta \in \Theta_{0} \]

is called the sampling distribution of the test statistic.

In many cases, \(T\) is constructed from the likelihood function \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta}(X)\), leading to the likelihood ratio statistic

\[ \Lambda(X)=\frac{\sup _{\theta \in \Theta_{0}} \mathbb{P}_{\theta}(X)}{\sup _{\theta \in \Theta} \mathbb{P}_{\theta}(X)}, \quad T(X)=-2 \log \Lambda(X) \]

Under suitable regularity conditions and for nested hypotheses, \(T(X)\) converges in distribution to a \(\chi_{k}^{2}\) random variable with \(k\) degrees of freedom equal to \(k=\operatorname{dim} \Theta-\operatorname{dim} \Theta_{0}\) (Wilks’ theorem).

20.1 p-values and rejection regions

A test is defined by a rejection region \(\mathcal{A} \subset \Omega\), with the null hypothesis rejected if \(X \in \mathcal{A}\). The size of the test is

\[ \sup _{\theta \in \Theta_{0}} \mathbb{P}_{\theta}(X \in \mathcal{A})=\alpha \]

where \(\alpha \in(0,1)\) is the prescribed significance level.

Equivalently, one computes the p-value associated with the observed data \(X_{\text {obs }}\),

\[ p\left(X_{\text {obs }}\right)=\sup _{\theta \in \Theta_{0}} \mathbb{P}_{\theta}\left(T(X) \geq T\left(X_{\text {obs }}\right)\right) \]

for a one-sided test (with obvious modifications for two-sided tests). The decision rule is

\[ \text { reject } H_{0} \quad \text { if } \quad p\left(X_{\text {obs }}\right)<\alpha \]

Unlike Bayesian model comparison, this procedure does not assign relative weights or posterior probabilities to competing models. Instead, it provides a binary decision based on whether the observed data fall into a region that is unlikely under \(H_{0}\).

20.2 Error control and power

Frequentist testing is characterized by explicit control of error probabilities under repeated sampling. A Type I error corresponds to rejecting \(H_{0}\) when \(\theta \in \Theta_{0}\), with probability bounded by \(\alpha\). A Type II error corresponds to failing to reject \(H_{0}\) when \(\theta \in \Theta_{1}\), with probability

\[ \beta(\theta)=\mathbb{P}_{\theta}(X \notin \mathcal{R}), \quad \theta \in \Theta_{1} \]

The power function of the test is defined as

\[ \pi(\theta)=\mathbb{P}_{\theta}(X \in \mathcal{R})=1-\beta(\theta) \]

and quantifies the sensitivity of the test to deviations from the null hypothesis. Optimal tests are often constructed by maximizing power subject to a fixed Type I error constraint, as formalized by the Neyman-Pearson lemma.

From this perspective, frequentist model comparison emphasizes long-run performance guarantees and controlled error frequencies, rather than degrees of belief or probabilistic statements about model validity.
