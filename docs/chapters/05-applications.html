15.1 Bayesian estimation of the Bernoulli parameter \(p\)

Consider a sequence of independent Bernoulli trials

\[ X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n} \sim \operatorname{Bernoulli}(p) \]

where the unknown parameter \(p \in[0,1]\) represents the probability of success. Given observed data

\[ \mathcal{D}=\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\right\}, \quad x_{i} \in\{0,1\} \]

let \(k=\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i}\) denote the number of successes. The correct likelihood function to consider based on the hypotheses is

\[ P(k \mid p, n, H)=\binom{n}{k} p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k} \propto \operatorname{Binomial}(n, p) \]

where we note that \(n\) is part of the data but is fixed, i.e., not a random variable.

We need to specifying a prior distribution \(\pi(p)\) over the parameter space. Different priors encode different levels of prior information or desiderata. A common non-informative choice is the uniform prior

\[ \pi(p)=\mathcal{U}(0,1) \]

which assigns equal weight to all values of \(p\). This prior corresponds to a \(\operatorname{Beta}(1,1)\) distribution and reflects prior ignorance about the success probability. We use it when no strong prior information is available or simplicity and interpretability are desired.

A more principled non-informative prior is the Jeffreys prior, defined as

\[ \pi_{J}(p) \propto \sqrt{I(p)}, \]

where \(I(p)\) is the Fisher information of the likelihood probability 1

\[ I(p)=\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial p} \log P(k \mid p, n, H)\right)^{2}\right]=\frac{1}{p(1-p)} \]

Thus,

\[ \pi_{J}(p)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}} \]

This corresponds to a \(\operatorname{Beta}\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right)\) distribution. The main advantages of a Jeffreys priors is that it is invariant under reparameterization 2 and it gives more weight near the boundaries \(p=0\) and \(p=1\), reflecting higher sensitivity of the likelihood there.

When prior information is available, one may choose a Beta prior

\[ p \sim \operatorname{Beta}(\alpha, \beta), \quad \pi(p)=\frac{1}{B(\alpha, \beta)} p^{\alpha-1}(1-p)^{\beta-1} \]

where \(\alpha, \beta>0\). Here, \(\alpha-1\) represents the prior number of successes while \(\beta-1\) is the prior number of failures. This makes the prior equivalent to observing \(\alpha+\beta-2\) pseudo-observations.

For a Beta prior Bayes’ theorem gives the posterior distribution

\[ P(p \mid k, n, H) \propto p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k} \times p^{\alpha-1}(1-p)^{\beta-1}=p^{k+\alpha-1}(1-p)^{n-k+\beta-1} \propto \operatorname{Beta}(\alpha+k, \beta+n-k) \]

The Beta distribution is conjugate to the Bernoulli likelihood, which allows the posterior to be obtained analytically and updated sequentially.

From the posterior distribution, we obtain

  • Posterior mean:

\[ \mathbb{E}[p \mid k, n, H]=\frac{\alpha+k}{\alpha+\beta+n} \]

  • Posterior mode (MAP estimate, if \(\alpha+k>1\) and \(\beta+n-k>1\)):

\[ p_{\mathrm{MAP}}=\frac{\alpha+k-1}{\alpha+\beta+n-2} \]

  • Posterior variance:

\[ \operatorname{Var}(p \mid k, n, H)=\frac{(\alpha+k)(\beta+n-k)}{(\alpha+\beta+n)^{2}(\alpha+\beta+n+1)} \]

As \(n \rightarrow \infty\), the posterior concentrates around the empirical frequency \(k / n\), independently of the prior (provided the prior is proper). This reflects Bayesian consistency and the dominance of data over prior information in the large-sample limit.

The log-posterior density (up to an additive constant) is

\[ \mathcal{L}(p)=(a-1) \log p+(b-1) \log (1-p) \]

with \(a=\alpha+k\) and \(b=\alpha+\beta+n\). Its second derivative

\[ \mathcal{L}^{\prime \prime}\left(p_{\mathrm{MAP}}\right)=-(a+b-2)^{2}\left(\frac{1}{a-1}+\frac{1}{b-1}\right) \tag{114} \]

which gives the reliability \(\left[\mathcal{L}^{\prime \prime}\left(p_{\text {MAP }}\right)\right]^{-1 / 2}\).

15.2 Histogramming events: frequentist, Bayesian, and large-deviation perspectives

We consider a collection of \(n\) independent and identically distributed data points taking values in an interval \([a, b]\). Fix a bin \(B=[u, v] \subset[a, b]\), and denote by \(X\) the number of data points falling into \(B\). Our question is: how do we estimate the error to associate to a bin count?

15.2.1 Frequentist picture

In the frequentist paradigm, the probability

\[ p=\mathbb{P}(\text { a single data point falls in } B) \tag{115} \]

is a fixed but unknown parameter, while \(X\) is a random variable. Under the assumption of independent sampling,

\[ X \sim \operatorname{Binomial}(n, p), \tag{116} \]

with

\[ \mathbb{E}[X]=n p, \quad \operatorname{Var}(X)=n p(1-p) . \tag{117} \]

A natural estimator of \(p\) is the empirical frequency

\[ \hat{p}=\frac{X}{n} \tag{118} \]

This estimator is unbiased, i.e., its expected value coincides with \(p\),

\[ \mathbb{E}[\hat{p}]=p \tag{119} \]

and it is also consistent, namely, the variance shrinks to zero as \(n\) increases,

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\hat{p})=\frac{p(1-p)}{n} \underset{n \rightarrow \infty}{\longrightarrow} 0 \tag{120} \]

After observing a realization \(X=x_{0}\), one typically replaces \(p\) by \(\hat{p}=x_{0} / n\) in the binomial formulas, yielding the plug-in estimates

\[ \widehat{\mathbb{E}}[X]=x_{0}, \quad \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(X)=x_{0}\left(1-\frac{x_{0}}{n}\right) \tag{121} \]

Note that in the Poisson limit \(n \rightarrow \infty(p \rightarrow 0)\), the plug-in variance reads \(\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(X)=x_{0}\), whose square root is the typical uncertainty associated to a bin value. These quantify sampling fluctuations under repeated experiments, without assigning any probability distribution to \(p\) itself.

15.2.2 Bayesian picture

In the Bayesian paradigm, the parameter \(p\) is treated as a random variable. One assigns a prior distribution, typically a Beta distribution,

\[ p \sim \operatorname{Beta}(\alpha, \beta) \tag{122} \]

Given an observed count \(X=x_{0}\), the likelihood is binomial and conjugacy yields the posterior

\[ p \mid x_{0} \sim \operatorname{Beta}\left(\alpha+x_{0}, \beta+n-x_{0}\right) . \tag{123} \]

The posterior mean is

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[p \mid x_{0}\right]=\frac{\alpha+x_{0}}{\alpha+\beta+n}, \tag{124} \]

which reduces to \(\left(x_{0}+1\right) /(n+2)\) for a uniform prior \((\alpha=\beta=1)\) and boils down to the frequency estimation only for \(x_{0}, n \gg 1\).

Predictions for future data are obtained from the posterior predictive distribution (113) with the likelihood and posterior being a Binomial and a Beta distribution, respectively.

Future bin count \(X_{\text {new }}\) based on \(n\) trials,

\[ X_{\text {new }} \mid x_{0} \sim \operatorname{Beta-Binomial}\left(n, \alpha+x_{0}, \beta+n-x_{0}\right) . \tag{125} \]

The beta-binomial distribution is the binomial distribution in which the probability of success at each of the trials is not fixed but randomly drawn from a Beta distribution. Its pdf reads

\[ \begin{align*} f(x \mid n, \alpha, \beta) & =\int_{0}^{1} \operatorname{Binomial}(x \mid n, p) \operatorname{Beta}(\alpha, \beta) d p \\ & =\binom{n}{x} \frac{1}{\mathrm{~B}(\alpha, \beta)} \int_{0}^{1} p^{x+\alpha-1}(1-p)^{n-x+\beta-1} d p \\ & =\binom{n}{x} \frac{\mathrm{~B}(x+\alpha, n-x+\beta)}{\mathrm{B}(\alpha, \beta)} \tag{126-128} \end{align*} \]

Its variance exceeds the frequentist plug-in variance, as it incorporates both binomial sampling noise and uncertainty about \(p\).

15.2.3 Large deviation and entropy picture

The full distribution of estimator, seen as the empirical frequency \(\hat{p}=X / n\), satisfies a large deviation principle: the probability of \(\hat{p}\) concentrates exponentially fast in \(n \rightarrow \infty\) around its mean value. Indeed, for large \(n\) we can derive

\[ \mathbb{P}(\hat{p}=y) \asymp \exp [-n D(y \| p)] \tag{129} \]

where

\[ D(y \| p)=y \log \frac{y}{p}+(1-y) \log \frac{1-y}{1-p} \tag{130} \]

is the Kullback-Leibler divergence (also called relative entropy) between Bernoulli distributions. The symbol \(\asymp\) in (129) signifies log equality in the limit \(n \rightarrow \infty\), namely,

\[ -\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{n} \ln \mathbb{P}(\hat{p}=y)=D(y \| p) \tag{131} \]

This results descends from the fact that \(X\) follows the Binomial distribution,

\[ \mathbb{P}(\hat{p}=y) \propto \mathbb{P}(X=n y)=\binom{n}{n y} p^{n y}(1-p)^{n(1-y)} \asymp e^{n H(y)+n y \ln p+n(1-y) \ln (1-p)}, \tag{132} \]

where the entropy, obtained by Stirling approximation of the binomial factor,

\[ H(y)=-y \log y-(1-y) \log (1-y) \tag{133} \]

counts the logarithmic number of sequences compatible with a given empirical frequency.

Expanding \(D(y \| p)\) around its minimum at \(y=p\) yields

\[ D(y \| p)=\frac{(y-p)^{2}}{2 p(1-p)}+\mathcal{O}\left((y-p)^{3}\right) \tag{134} \]

which reproduces the central-limit result

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\hat{p})=\frac{p(1-p)}{n} \tag{135} \]

15.3 Bayesian inference for the Poisson rate parameter \(\lambda\)

Consider a set of independent observations

\[ D=\left\{x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}\right\} \]

where each \(x_{i} \in \mathbb{N}_{0}\) is drawn from a Poisson distribution with unknown rate parameter \(\lambda>0\). The likelihood function is

\[ P(\lambda \mid D)=\prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\lambda^{x_{i}} e^{-\lambda}}{x_{i}!} \propto \lambda^{y} e^{-n \lambda} \tag{136} \]

The likelihood depends on the data only through the sufficient statistic 3

\[ y=\sum_{i=1}^{n} k_{i} \]

A commonly used but formally improper (because unnormalizable) choice is the uniform prior on \(\lambda\),

\[ \pi(\lambda) \propto 1, \quad \lambda \geq 0 \]

Combining this with the likelihood yields the posterior

\[ p(\lambda \mid D) \propto \lambda^{y} e^{-n \lambda} \tag{137} \]

which is a Gamma distribution,

\[ \lambda \mid D \sim \operatorname{Gamma}(y+1, n), \]

The posterior mean and variance are

\[ \mathbb{E}[\lambda \mid D]=\frac{y+1}{n}, \quad \operatorname{Var}(\lambda \mid D)=\frac{y+1}{n} \]

Compared to the maximum likelihood estimator \(\hat{\lambda}_{\text {MLE }}=y / n\), the posterior mean is slightly shifted due to the prior.

Another choice is Jeffreys prior, which is invariant under reparametrizations, defined as \(\pi_{J}(\lambda) \propto \sqrt{I(\lambda)}\), where \(I(\lambda)=\frac{1}{\lambda}\) is the Fisher information of the Poisson distribution. This leads to

\[ \pi_{J}(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{-1 / 2} \]

and a resulting posterior

\[ p(\lambda \mid D) \propto \lambda^{y-1 / 2} e^{-n \lambda} \tag{138} \]

corresponding to

\[ \lambda \left\lvert\, D \sim \operatorname{Gamma}\left(y+\frac{1}{2}, n\right) .\right. \]

The posterior mean is

\[ \mathbb{E}[\lambda \mid D]=\frac{y+\frac{1}{2}}{n} \]

Finally, one can take the conjugate prior for the Poisson, i.e., the Gamma distribution,

\[ \pi(\lambda)=\operatorname{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta), \quad \pi(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta \lambda} \]

This choice leads to a posterior of the same functional form,

\[ \lambda \mid D \sim \operatorname{Gamma}(\alpha+y, \beta+n) . \tag{139} \]

The posterior mean and variance are

\[ \mathbb{E}[\lambda \mid D]=\frac{\alpha+y}{\beta+n}, \quad \operatorname{Var}(\lambda \mid D)=\frac{\alpha+y}{(\beta+n)^{2}} \]

The hyperparameters \((\alpha, \beta)\) can be interpreted as encoding prior pseudo-counts and prior exposure time.

For finite \(n\), different priors lead to different posterior distributions and estimators, particularly when the total count \(y\) is small. The influence of the prior is most pronounced in the low-data regime, where it can regularize estimates and prevent unphysical results.

As the number of observations grows large, the likelihood becomes increasingly sharply peaked. In the limit \(n \rightarrow \infty\) with \(y / n \rightarrow \lambda_{0}\), the posterior distribution concentrates around \(\lambda_{0}\) for all reasonable priors,

\[ p(\lambda \mid D) \underset{N \rightarrow \infty}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{N}\left(\lambda_{0}, \frac{\lambda_{0}}{n}\right), \]

and the differences between priors become negligible. This illustrates a general manifestation of the Bernstein-von Mises theorem: with sufficient data, Bayesian inference becomes prior-independent and agrees with frequentist estimators.

15.4 Bayesian inference for the Gaussian mean and variance

Let \(D=\left\{x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}\right\}\) be independent samples drawn from a normal distribution with unknown mean \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}\) and variance \(\sigma^{2}>0\),

\[ p\left(x \mid \mu, \sigma^{2}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} \exp \left[-\frac{(x-\mu)^{2}}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right] \]

We define the empirical mean and the empirical variance

\[ \bar{x}=\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i}, \quad S=\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2} \]

which will serve as sufficient statistics in what follows.

15.4.1 Inference of the mean with known variance

Assume that \(\sigma^{2}\) is known and that only the mean \(\mu\) is to be inferred. The likelihood is

\[ p(D \mid \mu)=\frac{1}{\left(2 \pi \sigma^{2}\right)^{n / 2}} \exp \left[-\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-\mu\right)^{2}}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right] \propto \exp \left[-\frac{n}{2 \sigma^{2}}(\mu-\bar{x})^{2}\right] \tag{140} \]

where we wrote \(\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-\mu\right)^{2}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2}+n(\bar{x}-\mu)^{2}\) and isolated the term that depends on \(\mu\). This shows that the empirical mean is a sufficient statistic when the variance is known.

A common noninformative choice is the improper uniform prior, \(p(\mu) \propto 1\) that yields the Gaussian posterior,

\[ \mu \left\lvert\, D \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\bar{x}, \frac{\sigma^{2}}{n}\right)\right. \tag{141} \]

The posterior mean coincides with the frequentist’s maximum likelihood estimator. Note that the flat prior also coincides with the Jeffreys prior since the Fisher information of the Gaussian is independent of \(\mu\):

\[ -\frac{d^{2}}{d \mu^{2}} \ln p(D \mid \mu)=\frac{1}{\sigma^{2}} \tag{142} \]

Using the conjugate prior for the mean, which is itself Gaussian,

\[ p(\mu)=\mathcal{N}\left(\mu_{0}, \sigma_{0}^{2}\right) \]

returns a Gaussian posterior,

\[ \mu \left\lvert\, D \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\frac{\mu_{0} / \sigma_{0}^{2}+n \bar{x} / \sigma^{2}}{1 / \sigma_{0}^{2}+n / \sigma^{2}}, \frac{1}{1 / \sigma_{0}^{2}+n / \sigma^{2}}\right)\right. \tag{143} \]

As \(n \rightarrow \infty\), the influence of the prior vanishes and the posterior concentrates around \(\bar{x}\).

15.4.2 Inference of the variance with known mean

Assume now that \(\mu\) is known and only the variance \(\sigma^{2}\) is to be inferred. The likelihood is

\[ p\left(\sigma^{2} \mid D\right) \propto \frac{1}{\left(2 \pi \sigma^{2}\right)^{n / 2}} \exp \left[-\frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-\mu\right)^{2}\right] \propto \frac{1}{\sigma^{n}} \exp \left[-\frac{n}{2 \sigma^{2}}\left(S+(\bar{x}-\mu)^{2}\right)\right] \tag{144} \]

For the variance parameter, Jeffreys prior is

\[ p_{J}\left(\sigma^{2}\right) \propto \frac{1}{\sigma^{2}} \]

that gives an inverse-gamma distribution posterior,

\[ \sigma^{2} \mid D \sim \text { Inv-Gamma }\left(\frac{n}{2}, \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-\mu\right)^{2}\right) \tag{145} \]

The conjugate prior for \(\sigma^{2}\) is the inverse-gamma distribution 4,

\[ p\left(\sigma^{2}\right)=\operatorname{Inv-Gamma}(\alpha, \beta), \]

which gives a posterior

\[ \sigma^{2} \left\lvert\, D \sim \operatorname{Inv-Gamma}\left(\alpha+\frac{n}{2}, \beta+\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-\mu\right)^{2}\right) .\right. \tag{146} \]

15.4.3 Joint inference of mean and variance

When both \(\mu\) and \(\sigma^{2}\) are unknown, the likelihood is again (144). In order to handle the bivariate prior we use factorization as

\[ p\left(\mu, \sigma^{2}\right)=p\left(\mu \mid \sigma^{2}\right) p\left(\sigma^{2}\right) . \tag{147} \]

If we want to use reparametrization invariant priors, then we employ the Jeffreys prior previously identified.

The conjugate prior for the joint inference problem is the Normal-Inverse-Gamma distribution,

\[ \begin{align*} \mu \mid \sigma^{2} & \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\mu_{0}, \frac{\sigma^{2}}{\kappa_{0}}\right), \\ \sigma^{2} & \sim \operatorname{Inv-Gamma}\left(\alpha_{0}, \beta_{0}\right) . \tag{148-149} \end{align*} \]

The posterior is by definition Normal-Inverse-Gamma, with updated parameters

\[ \begin{align*} \kappa_{n} & =\kappa_{0}+n \\ \mu_{n} & =\frac{\kappa_{0} \mu_{0}+n \bar{x}}{\kappa_{0}+n} \\ \alpha_{n} & =\alpha_{0}+\frac{n}{2} \\ \beta_{n} & =\beta_{0}+\frac{n}{2} S+\frac{\kappa_{0} n}{2\left(\kappa_{0}+n\right)}\left(\bar{x}-\mu_{0}\right)^{2} . \tag{150-153} \end{align*} \]

Marginalizing over \(\sigma^{2}\), the posterior distribution of \(\mu\) is a Student-\(t\) distribution,

\[ p(\mu \mid D)=t_{2 \alpha_{n}}\left(\mu_{n}, \frac{\beta_{n}}{\alpha_{n} \kappa_{n}}\right) . \tag{154} \]

This heavy-tailed distribution reflects the additional uncertainty associated with estimating the variance.

As \(n \rightarrow \infty\), the joint posterior distribution concentrates around the maximum likelihood estimates

\[ \hat{\mu}_{\mathrm{MLE}}=\bar{x}, \quad \hat{\sigma}_{\mathrm{MLE}}^{2}=S, \]

Under standard regularity conditions on the prior (in particular, that it is positive and smooth in a neighborhood of the true parameter), the Bernstein-von Mises theorem implies that the posterior distribution becomes asymptotically Gaussian:

\[ p(\theta \mid D) \underset{n \rightarrow \infty}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{N}\left(\hat{\theta}, I_{n}^{-1}(\hat{\theta})\right), \]

where \(\hat{\theta}=\left(\hat{\mu}, \hat{\sigma}^{2}\right)\) is the maximum likelihood estimator (index dropped to lighten notation) and \(I_{n}(\theta)\) is the Fisher information matrix for \(n\) observations.

For a single observation \(x \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\mu, \sigma^{2}\right)\), the log-likelihood is

\[ \ell\left(\mu, \sigma^{2}\right)=-\frac{1}{2} \log \left(2 \pi \sigma^{2}\right)-\frac{(x-\mu)^{2}}{2 \sigma^{2}} \]

A straightforward calculation yields the Fisher information matrix for one observation:

\[ I_{1}\left(\mu, \sigma^{2}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{cc} \frac{1}{\sigma^{2}} & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{2\left(\sigma^{2}\right)^{2}} \end{array}\right) \]

Since the observations are independent, the Fisher information for \(N\) data points is additive:

\[ I_{n}\left(\mu, \sigma^{2}\right)=n I_{1}\left(\mu, \sigma^{2}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{cc} \frac{n}{\sigma^{2}} & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{n}{2\left(\sigma^{2}\right)^{2}} \end{array}\right) \]

The asymptotic covariance matrix of the posterior (or equivalently of the maximum likelihood estimator) is given by the inverse Fisher information calculated at \(\hat{\theta}\):

\[ I_{n}^{-1}\left(\hat{\mu}, \hat{\sigma}^{2}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{cc} \frac{\hat{\sigma}^{2}}{n} & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{2\left(\hat{\sigma}^{2}\right)^{2}}{n} \end{array}\right) \]

Thus, in the large-sample limit, the posterior factorizes asymptotically and the parameters \(\mu\) and \(\sigma^{2}\) become uncorrelated. Both uncertainties shrink as \(n^{-1 / 2}\), with

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\mu) \sim \frac{\hat{\sigma}^{2}}{n}, \quad \operatorname{Var}\left(\sigma^{2}\right) \sim \frac{2\left(\hat{\sigma}^{2}\right)^{2}}{n} \]

Importantly, this asymptotic result is independent of the specific choice of prior, provided the prior is regular.

15.4.4 Credible intervals

Credible intervals summarize posterior uncertainty about parameters.

Credible interval for the mean. We have

\[ \mu \left\lvert\, D \sim t_{2 \alpha_{n}}\left(\mu_{n}, \frac{\beta_{n}}{\alpha_{n} \kappa_{n}}\right)\right. \]

A central \((1-\alpha) \%\) credible interval for \(\mu\) is therefore

\[ \mu_{n} \pm t_{2 \alpha_{n}, 1-\alpha / 2} \sqrt{\frac{\beta_{n}}{\alpha_{n} \kappa_{n}}} \]

where \(t_{\nu, p}\) denotes the \(p\)-quantile of a Student-\(t\) distribution with \(\nu\) degrees of freedom. In the large-sample limit \(n \rightarrow \infty\), the Student-\(t\) converges to a Gaussian and the interval becomes

\[ \mu_{n} \pm z_{1-\alpha / 2} \sqrt{\frac{\hat{\sigma}^{2}}{n}} \]

recovering the familiar asymptotic confidence interval (see 112).

Credible interval for the variance. The marginal posterior of the variance is

\[ \sigma^{2} \mid D \sim \operatorname{Inv-Gamma}\left(\alpha_{n}, \beta_{n}\right) \]

Let \(q_{p}\) denote the \(p\)-quantile of a Gamma distribution with parameters \(\left(\alpha_{n}, \beta_{n}\right)\). Since \(1 / \sigma^{2}\) is Gamma-distributed, a central \(100(1-\alpha) \%\) credible interval for \(\sigma^{2}\) is

\[ \left[\frac{1}{q_{1-\alpha / 2}}, \frac{1}{q_{\alpha / 2}}\right] \]

For large \(n\), using asymptotic normality of the maximum likelihood estimator,

\[ \sigma^{2} \approx \hat{\sigma}^{2} \pm z_{1-\alpha / 2} \sqrt{\frac{2\left(\hat{\sigma}^{2}\right)^{2}}{n}} \]

Joint credible regions. In the large-sample limit the posterior is approximately Gaussian with covariance matrix \(I_{n}^{-1}(\hat{\theta})\). A joint \((1-\alpha) \%\) credible region for \(\theta=\left(\mu, \sigma^{2}\right)\) is given by the ellipse

\[ (\theta-\hat{\theta})^{T} I_{n}(\hat{\theta})(\theta-\hat{\theta}) \leq \chi_{2,1-\alpha}^{2} \]

15.4.5 One- and two-side hypothesis testing

Bayesian hypothesis testing compares posterior probabilities of competing hypotheses. The Gaussian model allows analytic expressions for several relevant cases.

One-sided hypothesis test for the mean. Consider

\[ H_{0}: \mu \leq \mu_{*}, \quad H_{1}: \mu>\mu_{*} \]

The posterior probability of \(H_{1}\) is

\[ P\left(H_{1} \mid D\right)=1-F_{t}\left(\frac{\mu_{*}-\mu_{n}}{\sqrt{\beta_{n} /\left(\alpha_{n} \kappa_{n}\right)}}\right) \]

where \(F_{t}\) denotes the cumulative distribution function of a Student-\(t\) distribution with \(2 \alpha_{n}\) degrees of freedom.

A simple decision rule is to reject \(H_{0}\) if

\[ P\left(H_{1} \mid D\right)>1-\alpha \]

Equivalently, \(H_{0}\) is rejected if \(\mu_{*}\) lies outside the lower bound of the corresponding credible interval.

Two-sided hypothesis test for the mean. Consider

\[ H_{0}: \mu=\mu_{*}, \quad H_{1}: \mu \neq \mu_{*} \]

A common Bayesian procedure is the credible-interval test: reject \(H_{0}\) if \(\mu_{*}\) lies outside the \((1-\alpha) \%\) credible interval for \(\mu\).

Alternatively, one may compare models via the Bayes factor

\[ \mathrm{BF}=\frac{p\left(D \mid M_{1}\right)}{p\left(D \mid M_{0}\right)} \]

where \(M_{0}\) corresponds to the restricted model \(\mu=\mu_{*}\) and \(M_{1}\) to the full model with the conjugate prior. The marginal likelihoods can be computed analytically in the conjugate Gaussian setting.

Testing the variance. For hypotheses on the variance such as

\[ H_{0}: \sigma^{2} \leq \sigma_{*}^{2}, \quad H_{1}: \sigma^{2}>\sigma_{*}^{2} \]

the posterior probability of \(H_{1}\) is

\[ P\left(H_{1} \mid D\right)=\int_{\sigma_{*}^{2}}^{\infty} \operatorname{Inv-Gamma}\left(\sigma^{2} ; \alpha_{n}, \beta_{n}\right) d \sigma^{2} \]

In the large-sample limit, this reproduces the classical \(\chi^{2}\)-type behavior obtained from asymptotic normality.

15.5 Fitting as a special case of parametric inference

Classical model fitting can be understood as a special case of parametric Bayesian inference, where a likelihood is assumed for the data and a prior is optionally specified. The choice of a loss function then determines the specific point estimate. Suppose we observe data

\[ D=\left\{\left(x_{i}, y_{i}\right)\right\}_{i=1}^{n} \]

and have a parametric model \(f(x ; \theta)\) with unknown parameters \(\theta \in \Theta\). To formalize fitting in a Bayesian framework, assume the observations are noisy realizations of the model:

\[ y_{i}=f\left(x_{i} ; \theta\right)+\epsilon_{i}, \quad \epsilon_{i} \sim \text { i.i.d. } \mathcal{N}\left(0, \sigma^{2}\right) \]

Then the likelihood of the data is

\[ p(D \mid \theta)=\prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2}}} \exp \left[-\frac{\left(y_{i}-f\left(x_{i} ; \theta\right)\right)^{2}}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right] \tag{155} \]

A prior \(p(\theta)\) encodes any previous belief about the parameters. In classical fitting, one usually assumes a uniform (improper) prior, corresponding to no preference, \(p(\theta) \propto 1\). Bayes’ theorem as usual gives the posterior distribution: combining the information from the data (likelihood) and prior.

If we are only interested in estimating a numerical value for the parameters, given a loss function \(L(\hat{\theta}, \theta)\), the Bayesian point estimate minimizes the posterior expected loss, as seen before. Using the \(0-1\) loss function leading to posterior mode with the likelihood (155) turns the MAP estimator into the least-squares fit

\[ \begin{aligned} \hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{MAP}} & =\arg \max _{\theta} p(\theta \mid D) \\ & =\arg \max _{\theta} p(D \mid \theta) \\ & =\arg \min _{\theta} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(y_{i}-f\left(x_{i} ; \theta\right)\right)^{2} \end{aligned} \]

where we used \(p(\theta \mid D) \propto p(D \mid \theta)\) for uniform priors. We have then show that classical least-squares fitting corresponds to a Gaussian likelihood, uniform prior, and \(0-1\) loss leading to the posterior mode (MAP estimate). Weighted least squares arises if the noise variance \(\sigma_{i}^{2}\) depends on \(i\), while other likelihoods (e.g., Poisson, Binomial) lead to generalized fitting procedures.
